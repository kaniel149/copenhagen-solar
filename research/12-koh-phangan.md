## 1. Electricity Infrastructure

### 1.1 Current Grid Connection

Koh Phangan's electricity supply is **entirely dependent on a single aging submarine cable** from the mainland. The island has no local generation capacity of significance — all power is transmitted undersea from Khanom, Nakhon Si Thammarat province via Koh Samui.

**Current system architecture:**
- Mainland generation → Khanom Substation → 115 kV submarine cables → Koh Samui → 33 kV distribution → Koh Phangan
- The island receives power stepped down from Koh Samui's grid
- No direct high-voltage submarine cable to Koh Phangan itself

**Existing cable system (Koh Samui regional):**
- Two 115 kV submarine circuits from mainland
- Two 33 kV submarine circuits (supplementary)
- Total installed capacity serving Ko Samui + Koh Phangan + Koh Tao: approximately 174 MW
- System is aging and increasingly unreliable

**Source:** EGAT press release on 230 kV upgrade; Canvas Solar analysis; Nation Thailand reporting.

### 1.2 The 115 kV Submarine Cable — History & Incidents

The existing 115 kV submarine cable system has experienced multiple failures:

- **Late December (major outage):** A rupture in the 30 km underwater high-voltage cable caused a **3-day blackout** across both Koh Phangan and Koh Samui. The failure was directly attributed to a **surge in demand during peak tourism season**.
  - Engineers worked overnight to restore power
  - The Surat Thani provincial governor declared a **disaster zone**
  - **50 million baht** in emergency funds allocated for food, water, and generators
  - Hospitals relied on backup generators
  - Telecoms, restaurants, and shops closed
  - Massive tourist exodus — estimated **12 billion baht in damages** (Koh Samui tourism chair estimate)

**Source:** Koh Phangan News (kohphangannews.org), reporting on engineers restoring power.

### 1.3 The 230 kV Submarine Cable Upgrade Project

This is the most significant infrastructure project planned for the islands:

| Parameter | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Project Name** | 230 kV Submarine Cable System — Khanom to Koh Samui |
| **Lead Agencies** | EGAT (transmission) + PEA (distribution) |
| **Route** | Khanom Substation (mainland) → Koh Samui |
| **Capacity** | 200 MW (new cables) |
| **Voltage** | 230 kV (replacing 115 kV system) |
| **Cabinet Approval** | February 28, 2023 |
| **Total Budget** | 11.23 billion baht (~US$320M) |
| **TOR Approval** | PEA board scheduled March 2025 |
| **Construction Start** | 2026 (possibly June 2025 if bidding proceeds quickly) |
| **First Circuit Completion** | June 2028 |
| **Full Completion** | June 2029 |
| **Total Duration** | 7-8 years |

**Technical specifications:**
- Eco-friendly installation using **water jetting burial** (3-5 m depth) to minimize marine ecosystem impact
- **Real-time monitoring** systems for cable health
- Two separate circuits for redundancy
- Designed to meet projected 200 MW demand by 2025 for the three islands

**Impact on Koh Phangan:**
- The upgrade primarily serves Koh Samui, but Koh Phangan will benefit from improved regional capacity
- Koh Phangan's sub-transmission from Koh Samui will remain at 33 kV distribution level
- A separate PEA project addresses the Koh Phangan → Koh Tao 33 kV link
- **No direct 230 kV cable to Koh Phangan is planned**
- Benefits won't arrive until 2028-2029 at earliest

**Interim measures:**
- EGAT has lent generators providing 10 MW extra power to the island system
- PEA plans new 200 MW underwater cable by next year (March deadline cited in news)

**Source:** EGAT official announcement; Thailand PRD; Nation Thailand; Global Transmission Report.

### 1.4 Koh Phangan to Koh Tao Cable

A separate project connects Koh Phangan to Koh Tao:

| Parameter | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Voltage** | 33 kV |
| **Distance** | 45 circuit km (physical ~21 km) |
| **Features** | Two automatic voltage regulators |

| Detail | Information |

| **Identifier** | Purple PEA sign |

| **PEA Website** | https://www.pea.co.th |

### 2.2 Distribution Network on the Island

- **Voltage levels:** 33 kV sub-transmission (from Koh Samui) → 22 kV distribution → 400/230V low voltage to consumers
- **Coverage:** Major roads and populated areas along the coast; interior/mountain areas have limited coverage
- **Infrastructure quality:** Overhead lines (concrete poles); exposed to storm damage
- **Smart grid:** None currently; basic metering infrastructure
- **Key distribution areas:**
  - Thongsala (main town/port) — highest connection density
  - Ban Tai — residential/tourism area
  - Haad Rin — Full Moon Party zone; high seasonal demand
  - Sri Thanu — growing wellness/expat community
  - Chaloklum — fishing village, developing tourism
  - Ban Khai — residential/resort area

### 2.3 Grid Connection for Solar

- PEA manages all new grid connections on the island
- Solar net metering / feed-in would require PEA approval
- Current grid **already exceeds reliable capacity even in low season**
- Adding significant solar generation would require grid reinforcement
- Community solar regulations (VSPP) may apply for projects up to 10 MW
- **Key challenge:** The island grid is a radial network (single feed), not a mesh — solar injection has limits before stability issues

**Source:** Canvas Solar analysis; PEA website; Thailand utility structure.

## 3. Peak Demand & Power Outages

### 3.1 Estimated Peak Demand

No official published figure exists for Koh Phangan's isolated peak demand. Based on available data:

| Metric | Estimate | Basis |
|--------|----------|-------|
| **Koh Phangan estimated peak** | 15-25 MW | Inference from tourism population, comparable islands |
| **Koh Tao peak (2009)** | 3.2 MW | OSTI/DOE study |
| **Koh Samui + Koh Phangan + Koh Tao total** | ~174 MW (system capacity) | EGAT submarine cable report |
| **Projected 2025 demand (3 islands)** | 200 MW | EGAT upgrade justification |

**Demand breakdown factors:**
- Approximately 15,000 permanent residents on Koh Phangan
- Tourist population swells to 30,000-50,000 during peak season (Dec-Feb)
- Full Moon Party adds 10,000-30,000 visitors for a single night monthly
- Resorts, guesthouses, restaurants, bars running AC and lighting 24/7
- Koh Samui absorbs ~70-75% of the 3-island demand

### 3.2 Power Outage Frequency & History

**Outage pattern:**
- **Regular blackouts:** 10 minutes to several hours; frequency increases during high tourist season
- **Low season:** Grid already exceeds reliable capacity even when tourism is lower
- **Major events:** Cable ruptures can cause multi-day blackouts

**Notable outages:**
- **3-day December blackout:** Submarine cable rupture; disaster zone declared; 12 billion baht estimated damages to Koh Samui tourism alone
- **Seasonal overloads:** Frequent brownouts during December-February peak
- **Monsoon damage:** October-December storms damage overhead lines, causing localized outages
- **Full Moon Party nights:** Localized strain on Haad Rin area distribution

**PEA assessment:** Grid is fragile; demand already exceeds reliable capacity; no significant improvements planned before 2028-2029 (when 230 kV cable completes).

**Source:** Canvas Solar; Koh Phangan News; EGAT.

### 3.3 Implications for Solar Development

The unreliable grid creates a **strong business case for solar + BESS:**
- Businesses and resorts already invest in diesel generators (high fuel cost)
- Solar + battery offers cheaper, cleaner backup
- Off-grid and hybrid systems already being installed (see Section 8)
- Grid-tied solar with battery backup addresses both self-consumption and outage resilience
- **Typical payback period:** 5 years for hybrid solar systems on Koh Phangan (Canvas Solar estimate)

## 4. Land Market

### 4.1 Overview

Koh Phangan's land market has experienced dramatic appreciation:
- **Pre-COVID:** Western sea-view land ~3 million THB per rai
- **Post-COVID (2024-2026):** Same land 8-10 million THB per rai — a **~300% increase**
- **10-year trend:** 400% overall price appreciation island-wide
- **Median island-wide:** ~19.1 million THB per listing; ~21,200 THB/sqm (FazWaz data)
- **Active listings:** 11-113 plots at any given time

### 4.2 Prices by Area

#### Thongsala (Main Town / Port)
- **Character:** Administrative and commercial center; port for ferries
- **Price range:** 6-12 million THB per rai (estimated from central area trends)
- **Availability:** Limited; mostly commercial/mixed-use land
- **Solar suitability:** Low — dense commercial area, limited open land

#### Ban Tai
- **Character:** Long beach, residential, tourism, accessible from Thongsala
- **Price range:** 4.4-17.6 million THB per rai
  - Affordable access plots: ~2,750 THB/sqm (~4.4M/rai)
  - Prestigious plots: ~11,000 THB/sq.wa (~27,500 THB/sqm, ~44M/rai equivalent for premium)
  - Average flat land: ~4.9 million THB/rai
- **Chanote:** Some available
- **Solar suitability:** Moderate — some flat areas, but tourism use competes

#### Sri Thanu
- **Character:** Wellness/yoga community, quieter west coast
- **Price range:** Entry-level from 1.77 million THB for small plots nearby (MaduaWan area, 8 plots listed)
  - Larger plots: 5-8 million THB per rai (estimated)
- **Solar suitability:** Moderate — less dense, some flat land available

#### Chaloklum
- **Character:** Fishing village, north coast, developing tourism
- **Price range:** ~16,125 THB/sq.wa for development plots (~40,300 THB/sqm, ~64.5M/rai equivalent for premium)
  - Inland/jungle: 3.8-5.5 million THB per rai (historical sales)
- **Solar suitability:** Moderate — northern exposure, some flat areas

#### Haad Rin (Full Moon Party area)
- **Character:** Southern tip; party zone; high-density tourism
- **Price range:** Premium — beachfront at 47,222+ THB/sq.wa (up to 118,000 THB/sqm)
- **Solar suitability:** Very Low — dense, tourism-only land use

#### Interior / Jungle / Mountain
- **Character:** Steep terrain, some flat plateaus, mixed title
- **Price range:** 3.8-6.5 million THB per rai for jungle land
- **Solar suitability:** Low to Moderate — terrain challenges, but cheapest land; need access roads

#### Sea View (Various Locations)
- **Character:** Premium elevated plots with Gulf views
- **Price range:** 8-10 million THB per rai (post-COVID); up to 16+ million for prime views
- **Solar suitability:** Low — high value for tourism/residential development; poor economics for solar

### 4.3 Land Market Summary for Solar

| Area | Price per Rai (THB) | Solar Suitability | Notes |
|------|---------------------|-------------------|-------|
| Ban Tai (inland) | 4.4-6.0M | Best option | Flat, accessible, moderate price |
| Sri Thanu (inland) | 5.0-8.0M | Good | Quiet area, less competition |
| Chaloklum (inland) | 3.8-5.5M | Good | Cheapest with access |
| Interior jungle | 3.8-6.5M | Challenging | Terrain, access, clearing needed |

**Recommendation for solar farm land:** Target inland Ban Tai or Chaloklum areas at 4-6 million THB per rai. For a 5 MW solar farm (~50 rai needed), budget 200-300 million THB for land alone.

## 5. Land Title & Foreign Ownership

### 5.1 Land Title Types on Koh Phangan

| Title Type | Description | Solar Suitability |
|------------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Chanote (Nor Sor 4 Jor)** | Full freehold title; GPS surveyed; highest security | Best — bankable, can be leased long-term |
| **Nor Sor 3 Gor** | Confirmed certificate of use; can be upgraded to Chanote | Good — requires upgrade for bank financing |
| **Nor Sor 3** | Certificate of use; less precise boundaries | Acceptable — requires more due diligence |
| **Sor Kor 1** | Possession certificate; cannot be legally sold or developed | Not suitable for solar investment |
| **National Park land** | Government owned; no private development | Prohibited |

**Chanote availability on Koh Phangan:**
- Limited — much of the island is national park or lower-grade titles
- Chanote land commands a significant premium (30-50% over Nor Sor 3)
- Some listings explicitly mention Chanote availability
- **Always verify title type before purchase/lease**

### 5.2 Foreign Ownership Workarounds

Thai law prohibits foreigners from owning land directly (Land Code Act B.E. 2497/1954). Legal structures for solar development:

#### Option A: Long-Term Lease (Emphyteutic Lease)
- **Structure:** 30-year lease, renewable twice (up to 90 years total)
- **Building:** Foreigner can own the building (solar infrastructure) freehold under Article 1336
- **Registration:** Must be registered at Land Department
- **Pros:** Simpler, lower cost, clear legal basis
- **Cons:** Not true ownership; 30-year base term may concern lenders
- **For Solar:** Acceptable — 25-year PPA fits within lease term

#### Option B: Thai Company Structure
- **Structure:** Thai limited company (foreigner holds up to 49% shares; Thai nationals hold 51%)
- **Company buys land:** Freehold Chanote title in company name
- **Compliance:** Thai shares must be genuine — nominee structures are illegal
- **Penalties:** Void purchase + criminal penalties for nominee arrangements
- **For Solar:** Preferred for large projects — gives company full land control
- **Cost:** Company setup ~50,000-200,000 THB; annual compliance costs

#### Option C: BOI Promoted Company
- **Structure:** BOI-promoted project allows foreign majority ownership (up to 100%)
- **Land:** BOI-promoted companies can own land for the promoted activity
- **For Solar:** **Best option** — solar power generation is BOI Category 7.1
- **Benefits:** Tax holidays, import duty exemptions, land ownership rights
- **Requirement:** Apply to BOI; meet minimum investment thresholds

#### Option D: Marriage to Thai National
- **Structure:** Land titled to Thai spouse; declaration that funds are solely theirs
- **For Solar:** Not practical for commercial projects
- **Risk:** Asset exposure in divorce

### 5.3 Key Legal Requirements

- **Foreign Exchange Transaction Form (FETF):** Required for land registration; proves funds came from abroad via Thai bank
- **Contracts:** Must be in Thai; require lawyer review
- **Due diligence:** Verify title type, encumbrances, access rights, zoning
- **Recommended lawyers:** See Topic 11 law firms section (Kudun, Formichella, Tilleke)

**Source:** Benoit Partners; Islanders Properties; Phangan Land & Home; Kohphangan.estate.

## 6. National Park Boundaries

### 6.1 Than Sadet–Ko Pha-ngan National Park

| Parameter | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Established** | November 22, 2018 (Royal Decree) |
| **Number** | Thailand's 132nd national park |
| **Area** | 26,866 rai (~43 km²) |
| **Island total** | ~65 km² (Koh Phangan island) |
| **Park coverage** | ~66% of the island |
| **Highest point** | Khao Ra — 635 meters |

### 6.2 Park Boundaries

The national park encompasses:
- **Central mountain forest** of Koh Phangan (the spine of the island)
- **Than Sadet valley** — 2.5-3 km cascade from central ridge to eastern bay (Ao Than Sadet)
- **Eastern/northeastern coastal zones**
- **Several offshore islets:** North and east of Koh Phangan, including Wao and Hin Bai archipelago
- **Boundary contours:** Published in the Royal Gazette

### 6.3 Impact on Solar Development

**Critical constraint:** With approximately **66% of the island designated as national park**, the developable area for solar is severely limited.

**What the park means for solar:**
- **No solar development permitted** within park boundaries — national park land is government-owned
- **Buffer zones** around park boundaries may have additional restrictions
- **EIA requirements** for any development near park boundaries
- **Remaining ~34% of island** (~22 km²) is where all development must occur
- This remaining area is already heavily utilized for tourism, residential, and commercial purposes

**Developable land breakdown (approximate):**
| Zone | Area (km²) | Status |
|------|-----------|--------|
| National Park | ~43 | No development allowed |
| Coastal tourism/residential | ~12-15 | Built up / high value |
| Inland flat (developable) | ~5-7 | Potential for solar |
| Agricultural | ~2-3 | Potential for solar (with conversion) |

**Realistic solar footprint:** Perhaps 2-3 km² (1,250-1,875 rai) of technically suitable, legally available, economically reasonable land exists for solar — assuming it's not already planned for other development.

**Source:** Wikipedia; Islanders Properties; Royal Gazette; Phanganist.

## 7. Tourism & Electricity Demand

### 7.1 Tourism Profile

Koh Phangan is one of Thailand's most popular islands, primarily known for:
- **Full Moon Party** — monthly event at Haad Rin beach; 10,000-30,000 attendees per event
- **Wellness/yoga retreats** — Sri Thanu, Ban Tai areas
- **Diving/snorkeling** — Sail Rock, Koh Ma
- **Digital nomad community** — growing year-round expat population

### 7.2 Seasonal Demand Pattern

| Period | Tourism Level | Electricity Impact |
|--------|--------------|-------------------|
| **Dec-Feb** (High Season) | Peak — 30,000-50,000 tourists | Maximum demand; grid stress; frequent outages |
| **Mar-Apr** (Hot Season) | High — 20,000-30,000 | High AC demand; heat amplifies consumption |
| **May-Sep** (Southwest Monsoon) | Low-Medium — 10,000-20,000 | Lower demand; some storm damage to lines |
| **Oct-Nov** (Northeast Monsoon) | Low — 5,000-15,000 | Lowest demand; but flooding/storm risks |

### 7.3 Full Moon Party Electricity Impact

The Full Moon Party creates a **monthly demand spike** centered on Haad Rin:
- **Sound systems:** Multiple stages with 10,000+ watt PA systems
- **Lighting:** LED installations, spotlights, UV/neon lighting across the beach
- **Bars/restaurants:** 50+ venues operating at peak capacity
- **Hotels/guesthouses:** 100% occupancy in Haad Rin and surrounding areas
- **Estimated additional load:** 2-5 MW above normal for the Haad Rin area during the event
- **Duration:** ~8 PM to ~8 AM (12 hours of peak demand overnight)
- **Frequency:** Monthly (aligned with full moon calendar)

### 7.4 Electricity Consumption Drivers

| Sector | % of Demand (est.) | Peak Driver |
|--------|-------------------|-------------|
| **Resorts/Hotels** | 40-50% | AC is dominant load; 24/7 operation |
| **Restaurants/Bars** | 15-20% | Refrigeration, cooking, lighting |
| **Residential (local)** | 15-20% | AC, cooking, basic appliances |
| **Commercial (shops)** | 5-10% | Lighting, refrigeration |
| **Events (FMP etc.)** | 5-10% (periodic) | Sound, lighting, temporary infrastructure |
| **Infrastructure** | 5% | Water pumping, street lights, telecoms |

### 7.5 Implications for Solar

- **Demand peaks in daytime** (AC load) align well with solar generation
- **Evening/night peaks** (tourism, events) require battery storage
- **Seasonal variation** creates financing challenge — lower demand during monsoon means lower revenue in those months
- **Tourism growth** ensures increasing demand — good for long-term solar investment
- **Grid unreliability** makes solar+BESS a premium product for resorts (willingness to pay for reliability)

## 9. Environmental Considerations

### 9.1 Than Sadet National Park
- **Coverage:** ~66% of the island (see Section 6)
- **Restrictions:** No development in park land; buffer zone considerations
- **Wildlife:** Diverse tropical species; protected under national park legislation
- **Waterways:** Than Sadet River (royal waterfall); several other watersheds
- **Significance:** Historical royal visits; cultural heritage site

### 9.2 Monsoon & Weather Patterns

| Season | Period | Rainfall | Wind | Solar Irradiance |
|--------|--------|----------|------|-----------------|
| **Northeast Monsoon** | Oct-Dec | Heavy (200-400mm/month) | NE 15-25 km/h | Low (3-4 kWh/m²/day) |
| **Cool Dry** | Jan-Feb | Light (50-100mm/month) | Variable | Moderate (4-5 kWh/m²/day) |
| **Hot Season** | Mar-May | Low-Moderate (100-200mm/month) | Variable | High (5-6 kWh/m²/day) |
| **Southwest Monsoon** | Jun-Sep | Moderate (150-250mm/month) | SW 10-20 km/h | Moderate (4-5 kWh/m²/day) |

**Annual average solar irradiance:** ~4.5-5.0 kWh/m²/day (Gulf of Thailand location)

**Key weather risks for solar:**
- **Monsoon rainfall:** October-December is the wettest period with potential for multi-day rain events
- **Short intense storms:** Year-round possibility of sudden downpours
- **High humidity:** Accelerates corrosion and panel soiling
- **Salt air:** Coastal environment requires marine-grade equipment

### 9.3 Flooding

**Pattern:**
- Primary flooding season: October-December (NE monsoon)
- Flash flooding from mountain runoff in central areas
- Coastal flooding during storm surges

**Notable flood events:**
- **December 2024:** 24+ hours nonstop rain; knee-deep street flooding; mudslides; power outages; sand/mud wash from mountains
- **November 2025:** Heavy monsoon rains; road flooding; sand-covered potholes; broken water pipes from debris

**Flood risk areas:**
- Low-lying coastal plains (Ban Tai flat areas)
- Valley bottoms receiving mountain runoff
- Road corridors that channel water
- Areas below steep slopes (landslide risk)

**Impact on solar development:**
- **Ground-mounted systems must be elevated** above flood level (minimum 1.5-2m clearance recommended)
- **Foundation design** must account for saturated soil conditions
- **Access roads** may be impassable during monsoon peaks
- **Drainage planning** critical — solar farms can alter water runoff patterns
- **Insurance considerations:** Flood coverage essential (see Topic 11)
- **Reference:** 2011 Thailand floods submerged solar farms on mainland — design must prevent this

### 9.4 Erosion & Landslides

- **Hill erosion:** Intense rain erodes hillsides; sand/mud deposited on roads and beaches
- **Landslides:** Triggered by heavy rain on steep terrain, especially deforested areas
- **Construction risk:** Solar farm construction (vegetation clearing, grading) can increase erosion
- **Mitigation:** Retain vegetation where possible; use piling foundations; install erosion control measures

### 9.5 Marine Environment

- **Coral reefs:** Protected under multiple regulations; submarine cable installation follows 1992 Cabinet rules
- **Marine park areas:** Waters around some islets are protected
- **Relevance to solar:** No direct impact on ground-mounted solar, but coastal development requires sensitivity
- **Floating solar potential:** Marine protected areas limit locations for any floating PV

### 9.6 Vegetation & Land Clearing

- **Coconut plantations:** Common on flat lowland areas; potential solar sites
- **Rubber plantations:** Some inland areas; conversion possible
- **Tropical forest:** Protected if within national park; clearing outside park may require permits
- **Environmental assessment:** May be required for clearing >10 rai for commercial development

### 9.7 Environmental Permitting Requirements

| Project Size | Requirements |
|-------------|--------------|
| **< 1 MW** | Basic PEA connection approval; local building permit |
| **1-10 MW (VSPP)** | ERC license; potentially Initial Environmental Examination (IEE) |
| **> 10 MW (SPP)** | Full EIA required from ONEP; ERC license; multiple agency approvals |
| **Near national park** | Additional scrutiny; buffer zone compliance; possible park superintendent objection |

## 10. Solar Development Feasibility Assessment

### 10.1 SWOT Analysis for Solar on Koh Phangan

**Strengths:**
- Unreliable grid creates strong demand for solar + BESS
- High electricity cost on island (diesel backup is expensive)
- Growing tourism/population ensures increasing demand
- Proven local installations (Canvas Solar, Canopy Village)
- Government pushing community solar nationwide
- Year-round solar irradiance (tropical latitude ~9.7°N)
- 5-year payback already demonstrated

**Weaknesses:**
- 66% of island is national park (severely limits land)
- High land costs (4-6M THB/rai minimum for suitable land)
- Limited grid capacity for feed-in (radial network)
- No 230 kV cable until 2028-2029
- Small total market (15,000 residents + seasonal tourists)
- Monsoon season reduces both solar output and construction windows
- Foreign ownership restrictions on land

**Opportunities:**
- Grid upgrade (230 kV) will improve reliability but also enable feed-in
- Community solar policy (1,500 MW national target) creates regulatory pathway
- Resort/hotel market willing to pay premium for reliable power
- BOI incentives for solar power generation
- Carbon credits / ESG value for tourism operators
- Rising tourism creates growing demand baseline
- Floating solar on reservoirs/ponds (if any suitable)

**Threats:**
- National park expansion could further restrict land
- Regulatory uncertainty (community solar rules still being finalized)
- Competition with higher-value land uses (tourism, residential)
- Flooding and monsoon damage to installations
- Cable rupture causing extended grid outages (loss of feed-in revenue)
- Local opposition to industrial-looking installations on tourist island

### 10.2 Recommended Development Approach

1. **Phase 1: Distributed C&I Solar (Immediate)**
   - Target resorts, hotels, and commercial buildings
   - Rooftop solar + BESS systems (10-100 kW each)
   - Private PPA or self-consumption model
   - No land purchase needed; use existing rooftops
   - Partner with Canvas Solar or Phangan Solar locally

2. **Phase 2: Community Solar Pilot (2026-2027)**
   - Secure 20-50 rai of flat inland Chanote land (Ban Tai or Chaloklum)
   - Develop 1-3 MW community solar farm under VSPP regulation
