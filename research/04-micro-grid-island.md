## 1. Thai Island Electricity Challenges

### 1.1 Geographic Context

Thailand has approximately **1,430 islands** along its coastline, split between the Gulf of Thailand (east) and the Andaman Sea (west). Many inhabited islands rely on either submarine cables from the mainland or local diesel generation — both with significant limitations.

### 1.2 Core Challenges

| Challenge | Description | Impact |
|-----------|-------------|--------|
| **Submarine cable fragility** | Single cable dependency; failures cause outages lasting hours to days | Unreliable power for residents and tourism industry |
| **Diesel dependency** | Remote islands rely on shipped diesel for generators | High cost (THB 10–20+/kWh actual generation cost), pollution, supply chain vulnerability |
| **Demand growth** | Rapid tourism development (resorts, villas) outpaces infrastructure | Grid overload, brownouts, especially peak season (Dec–Mar) |
| **Environmental sensitivity** | Islands are ecologically fragile (coral, marine parks) | Diesel spills, emissions conflict with eco-tourism positioning |
| **Logistics** | Fuel transport by boat, limited maintenance access | Higher O&M costs, longer repair times |
| **Limited land** | Small land area constrains ground-mount solar | Rooftop, floating, or multi-use installations needed |
| **Seasonal demand** | Tourism peak vs. low season creates load variability | Over-sized generation for peak, underutilized in low season |

### 1.3 Island Categories by Power Supply

| Category | Power Source | Examples | Population Range |
|----------|-------------|----------|-----------------|
| **Grid-connected** (submarine cable) | Mainland grid via undersea cable | Koh Samui, Koh Phangan, Koh Chang | 10,000–50,000+ |
| **Diesel-dependent** (scheduled) | Diesel gensets, 2–12 hours/day | Smaller Gulf and Andaman islands | 100–3,000 |
| **Hybrid pilot** | Solar + diesel + BESS | Koh Phaluai, Koh Mak Noi (planned) | 200–2,000 |
| **No electricity** | None or individual solar panels | Very remote small islands | <100 |

### 1.4 Koh Phangan / Koh Samui Case Study

The islands of **Koh Phangan** and **Koh Samui** illustrate the submarine cable challenge:

- Both depend on a **single submarine cable** from the mainland managed by PEA
- **Power outages** range from 10 minutes to several hours
- Frequency of outages has increased with development
- **Rapid growth** — new villas, resorts, and businesses — strains the aging cable
- PEA has confirmed **no major cable upgrade expected before 2030**
- Growing interest in **solar + battery backup** as a hedge against grid instability
- Typical payback for solar-battery hybrid on islands: approximately **5 years**

### 1.5 Koh Samui 230 kV Submarine Cable Project

EGAT is planning a major infrastructure upgrade for Koh Samui:

- **230 kV submarine cable** to replace existing lower-voltage connection
- Construction scheduled to **start in 2026**
- Will significantly improve reliability and capacity for Koh Samui
- Discussions include **energy storage systems** for additional grid security
- Does not address smaller surrounding islands

### 2.1 Current Submarine Cable Network

Thailand's submarine power cable network connects major islands to the mainland grid:

| Island(s) | Voltage | Length | Operator | Status |
|-----------|---------|--------|----------|--------|
| Koh Samui | 115 kV (upgrading to 230 kV) | ~25 km | EGAT/PEA | Operational; 230 kV planned 2026 |
| Koh Phangan | 33 kV | ~15 km (from Koh Samui) | PEA | Operational; fragile |
| Koh Chang | 22 kV | ~8 km | PEA | Operational |
| Koh Tao | 33 kV | ~65 km (recently installed) | PEA | Recently connected |
| Koh Lanta | 22 kV | ~3 km | PEA | Operational |
| Koh Samet | 22 kV | ~7 km | PEA | Operational |
| Phuket | 115 kV | Multiple mainland links | EGAT/PEA | Operational (Phuket is large island) |

### 2.2 Cable Economics

Submarine cable installation is extremely expensive:

| Factor | Typical Range | Notes |
|--------|--------------|-------|
| Installation cost | ฿30–100 million per km | Depends on voltage, seabed conditions |
| Maintenance | ฿2–5 million per year | Including inspections, repairs |
| Repair time | Days to weeks | Specialized vessels required |
| Lifespan | 25–40 years | Subject to anchor damage, corrosion |
| Failure risk | Moderate | Anchor drags, fishing, seismic activity |

### 2.3 Cable vs. Micro-Grid Decision Matrix

For islands evaluating whether to install a submarine cable or develop a micro-grid:

| Factor | Submarine Cable | Micro-Grid (Solar+BESS+Diesel) |
|--------|----------------|-------------------------------|
| **CAPEX** | Very high (distance-dependent) | Moderate (capacity-dependent) |
| **Reliability** | High (when working), catastrophic when failed | High (redundant sources) |
| **LCOE** | Low if short distance, high demand | Competitive for remote/small islands |
| **Energy independence** | No (dependent on mainland) | Yes (self-sufficient) |
| **Environmental** | Seabed disruption during install | Lower emissions than diesel alone |
| **Scalability** | Fixed capacity | Modular, expandable |
| **Maintenance** | Specialized, expensive | Local, more accessible |
| **Best for** | Large islands with high demand, close to mainland | Small/remote islands, <5 km cable uneconomic |

## 3. Koh Phaluai Micro-Grid — Thailand's First Island Green Grid

### 3.1 Project Overview

The **Koh Phaluai Off-Grid Micro-Grid** is Thailand's landmark island renewable energy project, developed by PEA under the **"Green Island" initiative**.

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Location** | Koh Phaluai, Surat Thani Province (near Koh Samui) |
| **Developer** | Provincial Electricity Authority (PEA) |
| **Contractors** | Italthai Engineering, SIFANG |
| **Total budget** | **฿172 million** (~US$5.4 million) |
| **Solar PV capacity** | **1 MW** |
| **Battery storage (BESS)** | **1.5–1.875 MWh** |
| **Diesel backup** | 600 kW (or 2 × 375 kVA gensets) |
| **Grid type** | Off-grid micro-grid (no mainland connection) |
| **Purpose** | 24/7 reliable power for island residents |
| **Significance** | Thailand's first purpose-built island micro-grid |

### 3.2 System Architecture

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                MICRO-GRID CONTROLLER             │
│         (Energy Management System)               │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│                                                  │
│  ┌──────────┐  ┌──────────┐  ┌──────────────┐  │
│  │  1 MW    │  │ 1.5 MWh  │  │  600 kW      │  │
│  │  Solar   │  │  BESS    │  │  Diesel Gen  │  │
│  │  PV      │  │  Li-ion  │  │  (backup)    │  │
│  └────┬─────┘  └────┬─────┘  └──────┬───────┘  │
│       │              │               │           │
│       └──────────────┼───────────────┘           │
│                      │                            │
│              ┌───────┴───────┐                    │
│              │   Converters  │                    │
│              │   & Switchgear│                    │
│              └───────┬───────┘                    │
│                      │                            │
│              ┌───────┴───────┐                    │
│              │  Distribution │                    │
│              │    Lines      │                    │
│              └───────┬───────┘                    │
│                      │                            │
│              ┌───────┴───────┐                    │
│              │   Island      │                    │
│              │   Consumers   │                    │
│              └───────────────┘                    │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### 3.3 Key Components

| Component | Specification | Role |
|-----------|--------------|------|
| **Solar PV array** | 1 MW (ground-mounted) | Primary generation during daylight |
| **BESS (batteries)** | 1.5–1.875 MWh lithium-ion | Store excess solar, provide evening/night power |
| **Diesel generators** | 600 kW total | Backup for extended cloudy periods, peak demand |
| **Converters/inverters** | Grid-forming capable | Create stable AC grid without mainland reference |
| **Control building** | On-site | Houses EMS, monitoring, switchgear |
| **Distribution lines** | Low voltage | Connect to island households and businesses |

### 3.4 Academic Optimization Study (HOMER Pro)

A study using HOMER Pro software analyzed the Koh Phaluai system and proposed an optimized design:

| Parameter | PEA Design | Optimized Design |
|-----------|-----------|-----------------|
| Solar PV | 1 MW | **1.25 MW** |
| BESS | 1.5 MWh | **1.925 MWh** |
| Diesel generator | 600 kW | **300 kW** |
| Converter | Not specified | **500 kW** |
| LCOE | Not published | **$0.393/kWh** |
| CO2 emissions | Not published | **121,000 kg/year** |
| Renewable fraction | Not published | **89.6%** |
| Net present cost | ฿172M (~$5.4M) | **$5.75 million** |

Key findings:
- Higher solar + larger battery → significantly higher renewable fraction
- Smaller diesel generator sufficient when battery provides peak shaving
- LCOE of $0.393/kWh competitive against diesel-only generation ($0.50–0.80/kWh)
- CO2 reduction of ~80% compared to diesel-only scenario

### 3.5 Lessons Learned

1. **Grid-forming inverters essential** — Island micro-grids need inverters that can create their own frequency/voltage reference (unlike grid-following inverters on mainland)
2. **Diesel cannot be eliminated entirely** — Multi-day cloudy periods require backup
3. **Battery sizing is critical** — Undersized BESS means more diesel runtime; oversized increases CAPEX
4. **Community engagement** — Koh Phaluai succeeded partly due to strong community involvement
5. **Tropical conditions** — High temperatures reduce battery life; thermal management essential
6. **Saltwater environment** — Corrosion protection needed for all equipment

## 4. Koh Tao — Wave Energy + Renewables

### 4.1 Island Context

Koh Tao is a small island in Surat Thani Province, famous for diving tourism. It has undergone a significant energy transition:

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Location** | Gulf of Thailand, ~65 km from Koh Samui |
| **Population** | ~3,000 permanent + ~5,000 tourists in peak |
| **Previous power** | Diesel generators (expensive, noisy, polluting) |
| **Current power** | **New submarine cable** from mainland (PEA) |
| **Renewable pilots** | Wind turbine (under construction), wave energy (planned) |

### 4.2 Submarine Cable Connection (Recent)

PEA recently installed a submarine cable connecting Koh Tao to the mainland grid, replacing diesel dependency. This is a significant shift:

- **33 kV submarine cable** connecting to mainland
- Replaced previous diesel-only generation
- Dramatically reduced electricity costs for residents
- Improved reliability (when cable is functioning)
- Still vulnerable to cable failures (single point of failure)

### 4.3 IsWEPT Wave Energy Micro-Grid (Planned)

A consortium is developing a wave-powered micro-grid pilot on Koh Tao:

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Project name** | IsWEPT (Island Sustainable Wave Energy Power for Thailand) |
| **Consortium** | Eco Wave Power, Toshiba, Aquatera, AIT, Queen Mary University of London, Leonics, Hitachi Energy |
| **Funding** | £1.5 million grant from UK Innovate UK (Round 10) |
| **Technology** | Onshore wave energy converters (WECs) |
| **Energy management** | AI-based EMS, predictive controls, radar wave forecasting |
| **Storage** | Wireless distributed EMS (no lithium-ion batteries) |
| **Operator** | PEA |
| **Status** | Mid-stage simulation and trials (workshops held June 2025) |

**Key innovations:**
- Uses **onshore wave energy converters** — mounted on coastal structures, not offshore
- **AI-based energy management** with predictive controls
- **Radar wave forecasting** for production planning
- **No lithium-ion batteries** — uses alternative distributed energy management
- Low tariffs for residents — comparable to mobile phone bills
- Designed for replication across SE Asian island communities

### 4.4 Wind Energy on Koh Tao

- A **large wind turbine** is being constructed on a hill behind Chalok Ban Kao
- Represents early wind energy exploration for the island
- Supplements submarine cable power supply
- Community reaction mixed (visual impact concerns)

### 4.5 Relevance to Copenhagen Solar

The Koh Tao case demonstrates:
- Islands are actively seeking renewable alternatives
- Multiple technology approaches being tested (wave, wind, solar)
- International funding available (UK Innovate UK, GIZ)
- PEA is receptive to partnership with private sector
- Even cable-connected islands want renewable backup

## 5. Hybrid Solar + Diesel + BESS System Design

### 5.1 System Architecture Overview

The standard hybrid micro-grid design for Thai islands combines three power sources with intelligent management:

```
                    ┌──────────────┐
                    │     Solar    │
                    │   PV Array   │
                    │  (Primary)   │
                    └──────┬───────┘
                           │ DC
                    ┌──────┴───────┐
                    │   Solar      │
                    │   Inverter   │
                    └──────┬───────┘
                           │ AC
         ┌─────────────────┼─────────────────┐
         │                 │                  │
  ┌──────┴───────┐  ┌──────┴───────┐  ┌──────┴───────┐
  │   Battery    │  │    AC Bus    │  │   Diesel     │
  │   Inverter   │  │  (Main Grid) │  │   Generator  │
  │  (Bi-dir)    │  │              │  │  (Backup)    │
  └──────┬───────┘  └──────┬───────┘  └──────────────┘
         │                 │
  ┌──────┴───────┐  ┌──────┴───────┐
  │    BESS      │  │    Loads     │
  │  (Li-ion)    │  │  (Island)    │
  └──────────────┘  └──────────────┘
```

### 5.2 Component Sizing Rules of Thumb

For Thai island conditions (tropical, high irradiance ~1,600–1,800 kWh/m²/year):

| Component | Sizing Rule | Rationale |
|-----------|------------|-----------|
| **Solar PV** | 1.5–2.5× peak demand (kW) | Oversize to charge batteries and cover cloud variability |
| **BESS** | 4–8 hours of average load (kWh) | Cover evening/night + cloudy morning |
| **Diesel generator** | 0.8–1.0× peak demand (kW) | Full backup capability |
| **Converter** | Match BESS power rating | Enable full charge/discharge rate |

### 5.3 Dispatch Strategy

The Energy Management System (EMS) prioritizes sources in this order:

| Priority | Source | Condition |
|----------|--------|-----------|
| 1 | **Solar PV** | Always first when available |
| 2 | **Battery discharge** | When solar insufficient, battery SOC > minimum |
| 3 | **Diesel generator** | When battery SOC < threshold or demand exceeds solar + battery |
| 4 | **Load shedding** | Emergency only — disconnect non-critical loads |

**Battery management:**
- Charge from solar excess during daytime
- Discharge to serve load from sunset to sunrise
- Maintain minimum SOC (typically 20%) to preserve battery life
- Diesel charges battery only in emergency (low SOC + no solar forecast)

### 5.4 Key Design Parameters for Thai Islands

| Parameter | Typical Value | Notes |
|-----------|--------------|-------|
| **Solar irradiance** | 1,600–1,800 kWh/m²/year | Higher in south Thailand (Andaman) |
| **Peak sun hours** | 4.5–5.5 hours/day | Annual average |
| **Ambient temperature** | 28–35°C | Panel derating ~10–15% |
| **Humidity** | 70–90% RH | Corrosion concern |
| **Wind speed** | 3–7 m/s average | Coastal, higher during monsoon |
| **Salt spray zone** | 0–500m from coast | IP65+ rating required |
| **Typhoon risk** | Low–moderate (Gulf), moderate (Andaman) | Panel tilt and mounting critical |
| **Monsoon season** | May–Oct (SW), Nov–Feb (NE Gulf) | Reduced solar, increased wind |

### 5.5 Equipment Specifications for Island Conditions

| Equipment | Standard Spec | Island Enhancement |
|-----------|--------------|-------------------|
| **Solar panels** | Tier 1 mono/bifacial | Marine-grade frames, salt-mist certified (IEC 61701) |
| **Inverters** | Grid-forming, IP65 | Conformal coated PCBs, dehumidifiers |
| **BESS** | LFP chemistry preferred | HVAC-controlled enclosure, 20–25°C target |
| **Diesel genset** | Tier 4 emissions | Marine-grade, dual-fuel option |
| **Switchgear** | Outdoor rated | Stainless steel enclosures, epoxy-coated busbars |
| **Cables** | XLPE armored | UV-resistant, rodent-proof, marine-rated |
| **Mounting** | Hot-dip galvanized steel | Additional zinc coating, stainless fasteners |

### 5.6 Renewable Fraction Targets

| Scenario | Renewable Fraction | Diesel Savings | Notes |
|----------|-------------------|----------------|-------|
| **Conservative** | 60–70% | 50–60% | Smaller battery, reliable diesel backup |
| **Moderate** | 75–85% | 65–80% | Balanced economics and clean energy |
| **Aggressive** | 85–95% | 80–90% | Large battery, minimal diesel |
| **100% renewable** | 100% | 100% | Not practical without multi-day storage |

## 6. GIZ Island Hybrid Mini-Grid Projects

### 6.1 Program Overview

The **GIZ "Renewable Energy Hybrid Grid System for Thai Islands"** project, supported by the Rockefeller Foundation, developed feasibility studies and designs for hybrid mini-grids on two Thai islands.

### 6.2 Koh Mak Noi

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Location** | Gulf of Thailand |
| **Partner** | PEA (as alternative to planned submarine cable) |
| **Previous power** | Diesel generators, limited hours/day |
| **Optimized system** | 400 kWp Solar PV + 700 kWh Li-ion BESS + 3 × 80 kW diesel |
| **Converter** | 155 kW inverter |
| **Design approach** | LCOE minimization, all components at single power station |
| **PEA context** | Evaluated as alternative to submarine cable planned for 2019–2021 |

**Key design decisions:**
- All components co-located at single power station to minimize connections and losses
- Solar PV priority dispatch with diesel/battery filling gaps
- Battery stabilizes fluctuating solar output
- Generator load ranges maintained within efficient operating band
- Financial optimization via LCOE minimization

### 6.3 Koh Bulon Don

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Location** | Andaman Sea (Satun Province) |
| **Partner** | Private company (commercially viable model) |
| **Previous power** | Limited/no centralized electricity |
| **Optimized system** | 52.14 kWp Solar PV + 100 kWh Li-ion BESS + 3 × 15 kW diesel |
| **Converter** | 27.3 kW (46 kW AC string inverter for PV) |
| **Emphasis** | Community involvement, long-term operation |
| **Lesson** | Avoid past failed "donation" projects — commercial viability essential |

### 6.4 Methodology

GIZ's approach for both islands included:

1. **Household surveys** — Map electricity access, current costs, willingness to pay
2. **GPS mapping** — Identify potential solar PV sites, distribution routes
3. **Community consultation** — Understand needs, concerns, usage patterns
4. **Load profiling** — Measure daily/seasonal demand curves
5. **Solar resource assessment** — On-site irradiance measurement
6. **Simulation & optimization** — HOMER Pro or equivalent software
7. **Financial modeling** — LCOE comparison: hybrid vs. diesel-only vs. submarine cable
8. **Sensitivity analysis** — Diesel price scenarios, solar degradation, demand growth

### 6.5 Key Findings from GIZ Projects

- **Hybrid systems achieve lower LCOE** than pure diesel generation
- **24/7 power supply** is achievable with proper battery sizing
- **Community engagement is critical** — past donated systems failed without local ownership
- **Submarine cable is not always the best option** — hybrids can be cheaper for remote/small islands
- **Commercial viability** essential for long-term sustainability
- **Comparable to Tokelau results** — GIZ projects elsewhere achieved 82–84% diesel savings

### 6.6 LCOE Comparison (Estimated)

| Scenario | LCOE (THB/kWh) | Notes |
|----------|----------------|-------|
| Diesel only (island) | 15–25 | Depends on fuel transport cost |
| Hybrid solar+diesel+BESS | 10–16 | GIZ-optimized designs |
| Submarine cable (short <5 km) | 5–8 | Including cable CAPEX amortization |
| Submarine cable (long >20 km) | 12–20 | Distance-dependent |
| Mainland grid (reference) | 3.5–4.5 | Standard PEA tariff |

## 7. PEA Island Operations

### 7.1 PEA's Role on Islands

The **Provincial Electricity Authority (PEA)** is responsible for electricity distribution throughout Thailand outside the Bangkok metropolitan area, including all inhabited islands.

**PEA island responsibilities:**
- Operate and maintain submarine cables
- Run diesel power stations on off-grid islands
- Distribute electricity to island consumers
- Apply **uniform tariff policy** (island rates same as mainland)
- Absorb the cost difference between island generation and tariff revenue
- Plan and execute grid extensions to islands

### 7.2 Uniform Tariff Policy

Thailand applies a **uniform tariff** across the country, meaning island consumers pay the same rate as mainland consumers (~THB 4.26/kWh residential) regardless of actual generation cost.

**Implications:**
- PEA subsidizes island electricity at significant loss
- Actual diesel generation cost may be THB 10–20+/kWh on remote islands
- Subsidy creates perverse incentive (no price signal for conservation)
- Makes renewable investment ROI calculation complex — savings accrue to PEA, not consumers
- **Opportunity for PEA partnerships**: PEA strongly motivated to reduce island diesel costs

### 7.3 PEA Island Infrastructure

| Aspect | Typical Setup |
|--------|--------------|
| **Generation** | Diesel gensets (100 kW – 5 MW per island) |
| **Distribution** | Low voltage (400V), limited medium voltage |
| **Operating hours** | 2–24 hours/day depending on island size |
| **Staffing** | 2–10 PEA staff per island station |
| **Fuel supply** | Regular boat delivery from mainland depot |
| **Fuel storage** | On-island tank farm (5,000–50,000 liters) |

### 7.4 PEA's Openness to Partnerships

PEA has demonstrated willingness to work with:
- **Private companies** (Banpu NEXT on Koh Lipe)
- **International organizations** (GIZ on Koh Mak Noi)
- **Academic institutions** (AIT on Koh Tao IsWEPT)
- **Foreign consortiums** (Eco Wave Power consortium)

This openness is driven by the **financial pressure** of island diesel subsidies and Thailand's renewable energy targets.

## 8. Smart Grid Pilots — EGAT Mae Hong Son

### 8.1 Project Overview

While not an island project, the **EGAT Mae Hong Son Smart Grid Pilot** is Thailand's most advanced micro-grid implementation and provides critical lessons for island deployment.

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Location** | Mae Hong Son Province (northern Thailand) |
| **Developer** | EGAT |
| **Contractor** | Italthai Engineering |
| **Solar PV** | **3 MW** |
| **BESS** | **4 MW / 4 MWh** (estimated) |
| **Hydro** | Mae Sa Nga (10.34 MW) — existing |
| **Contract signed** | June 1, 2022 |
| **COD** | **May 25, 2023** |
| **Grid status** | Connected but geographically isolated (no direct transmission lines due to national parks) |

### 8.2 Why Mae Hong Son?

- **Geographically isolated** — surrounded by mountains and national parks
- **No direct transmission line** from main grid
- **Frequent blackouts** before smart grid
- Power supplied through long, vulnerable distribution lines
- **High renewable potential** — solar, hydro, biomass available
- **Similar challenges to islands** — isolation, limited grid, backup dependency

### 8.3 System Components

| Component | Specification | Function |
|-----------|--------------|----------|
| **Solar PV** | 3 MW ground-mount | Primary renewable generation |
| **BESS** | 4 MW power / ~4 MWh energy | Grid stabilization, peak shaving, renewable firming |
| **Hydro** | Mae Sa Nga 10.34 MW | Baseload + peaking |
| **Microgrid Control Center** | SCADA + EMS | Balances generation and consumption across all plants |
| **EV charging** | EleX by EGAT | Part of Green Tourism City vision |
| **Distribution** | Upgraded local grid | Smart metering, fault detection |

### 8.4 Achievements

- **Commercial operation** achieved May 25, 2023
- **Eliminated frequent blackouts** in Mae Hong Son
- **Renewable integration** — balances solar variability with hydro and BESS
- **Green Tourism City** — EV buses, charging stations, energy learning center
- **R&D hub** — Positioned as ASEAN smart grid leadership project
- **Part of Thailand's Smart Grid Master Plan** (2015–2036)

### 8.5 Lessons for Island Micro-Grids

| Lesson | Application to Islands |
|--------|----------------------|
| **Microgrid Control Center is essential** | Islands need centralized EMS for multi-source dispatch |
| **BESS enables high renewable fraction** | 4 MW BESS at Mae Hong Son proves utility-scale storage works in Thai conditions |
| **Community benefit integration** | Tourism, education, economic development boost acceptance |
| **Italthai Engineering capability** | Proven Thai contractor for micro-grid projects |
| **EGAT partnership model** | EGAT willing to invest in innovative grid solutions |

### 8.6 PEA Micro-Grid in Mae Sariang

Separate from EGAT's project, PEA has its own micro-grid pilot in **Mae Sariang District** (also in Mae Hong Son Province):
- Demonstrates PEA's parallel smart grid development
- Focuses on distribution-level micro-grid management
- Smaller scale than EGAT project
- Tests PEA's capability for future island micro-grid deployment

## 9. Diesel Costs on Islands

### 9.1 Current Diesel Fuel Pricing

| Parameter | Value (March 2026) |
|-----------|-------------------|
| **Mainland diesel price** | ~THB 30.56/liter (~$0.97/liter) |
| **Island diesel premium** | +10–30% (transport costs) |

| **Diesel consumption (genset)** | 0.25–0.35 liters/kWh |
| **Fuel cost per kWh** | THB 8.25–14.00/kWh (fuel only) |

### 9.2 True Cost of Diesel Generation on Islands

The total cost includes far more than fuel:

| Cost Component | THB/kWh | % of Total |
|---------------|---------|-----------|
| **Fuel** | 8.25–14.00 | 50–60% |
| **Generator O&M** | 1.50–3.00 | 10–15% |
| **Fuel transport** | 1.00–3.00 | 5–15% |
| **Staff/labor** | 1.00–2.00 | 5–10% |
| **Generator replacement** | 0.50–1.50 | 3–8% |
| **Fuel storage** | 0.30–0.80 | 2–5% |
| **Environmental compliance** | 0.20–0.50 | 1–3% |
| **TOTAL** | **12.75–24.80** | 100% |

### 9.3 Comparison with Solar Hybrid LCOE

| Generation Type | LCOE (THB/kWh) | Renewable % | CO2 (kg/kWh) |
|----------------|----------------|-------------|--------------|
| **Diesel only** | 12.75–24.80 | 0% | 0.65–0.80 |
| **Solar + diesel + BESS (moderate)** | 10–16 | 70–85% | 0.10–0.25 |
| **Solar + diesel + BESS (optimized)** | 8–14 | 85–95% | 0.03–0.10 |
| **Mainland grid (reference)** | 3.50–4.50 | ~30% RE | 0.45 |

### 9.4 PEA Subsidy Gap

Since PEA charges uniform tariff (~THB 4.26/kWh) but actual island generation costs THB 12–25/kWh:

| Island Type | Generation Cost | Tariff Revenue | PEA Loss per kWh |
|-------------|----------------|----------------|-------------------|
| Small remote (diesel only) | THB 20–25 | THB 4.26 | THB 15.74–20.74 |
| Medium (diesel) | THB 12–18 | THB 4.26 | THB 7.74–13.74 |
| Cable-connected | THB 5–8 | THB 4.26 | THB 0.74–3.74 |

**This subsidy gap is PEA's strongest motivation for solar hybrid adoption** — every kWh of diesel replaced by solar saves PEA THB 8–20.

## 12. Design Considerations for Island Micro-Grids

### 12.1 Grid-Forming vs. Grid-Following Inverters

| Characteristic | Grid-Following | Grid-Forming |
|---------------|---------------|-------------|
| **Creates frequency/voltage** | No (follows grid) | **Yes (creates grid)** |
| **Requires grid reference** | Yes | No |
| **Island operation** | Not suitable | **Required** |
| **Black start capable** | No | **Yes** |
| **Cost** | Lower | Higher |
| **Recommendation** | Mainland solar farms | **All island micro-grids** |

### 12.2 Battery Chemistry Selection

| Chemistry | Energy Density | Cycle Life | Safety | Cost | Island Suitability |
|-----------|---------------|-----------|--------|------|-------------------|
| **LFP (LiFePO4)** | Medium | 4,000–6,000 | Excellent | Moderate | **Recommended** |
| **NMC** | High | 2,000–4,000 | Good | Lower | Acceptable |
| **Flow (Vanadium)** | Low | 10,000+ | Excellent | High | Good for large systems |
| **Lead-acid** | Low | 800–1,500 | Fair | Low | Budget only |
| **Sodium-ion** | Medium | 3,000+ | Good | Declining | Emerging option |

**LFP recommended for islands** due to:
- Superior thermal stability (important in tropical heat)
- Longer cycle life
- No thermal runaway risk
- Better saltwater corrosion resistance
- Lower maintenance

### 12.3 Corrosion Protection

| Zone | Distance from Sea | Protection Required |
|------|-------------------|-------------------|
| **Splash zone** | 0–50m | Marine-grade stainless steel, sealed enclosures |
| **Salt spray zone** | 50–500m | Hot-dip galvanized + epoxy coating |
| **Mild zone** | 500m–2km | Standard galvanized + weather protection |
| **Inland** | >2km | Standard protection |

### 12.4 Logistics and Installation

| Challenge | Solution |
|-----------|----------|
| **Equipment transport** | Barge or ferry; plan for low-tide draft limits |
| **Heavy lifting** | Mobile crane on barge or shore crane |
| **Construction workers** | Camp on island or daily ferry |
| **Materials** | Pre-fabricate on mainland where possible |
| **Commissioning** | Remote monitoring capability essential for ongoing support |
| **Spares** | Keep critical spares on-island (inverter boards, fuses, batteries) |

### 12.5 Typical Project Timeline (Island Micro-Grid)

| Phase | Duration | Activities |
|-------|----------|-----------|
| **Feasibility** | 2–3 months | Site survey, load profiling, solar assessment |
| **Design** | 2–3 months | System design, EMS specification, permitting |
| **Procurement** | 3–4 months | Equipment sourcing, shipping |
| **Civil works** | 2–3 months | Foundations, control building, cable trenching |
| **Installation** | 2–3 months | Solar, BESS, diesel, switchgear, distribution |
| **Commissioning** | 1–2 months | Testing, EMS tuning, grid stabilization |
| **Total** | **12–18 months** | From feasibility to COD |
