## 1. Electricity Sector Structure

### 1.1 The Enhanced Single Buyer (ESB) Model

Thailand's electricity sector operates under an **Enhanced Single Buyer (ESB)** model — a legal monopoly managed by three state-owned utilities. This structure is critical to understand for any solar developer, as it determines who you sell power to and how.

**Key Principle:** There is no open competitive electricity market in Thailand. EGAT acts as the sole buyer of bulk power, and PEA/MEA are the sole distributors to end consumers.

### 1.2 EGAT — Electricity Generating Authority of Thailand

**Role:** Central planner, generator, bulk buyer, and transmission operator

EGAT is the backbone of Thailand's power sector. Its responsibilities include:

- **Generation:** Operates 54 power plants with a total installed capacity of **16,261 MW** (as of 2024), including thermal, combined cycle, hydropower, wind, and solar facilities
- **Bulk Purchasing:** Purchases power from:
  - 13 Independent Power Producers (IPPs): **19,598 MW**
  - Small Power Producers (SPPs): **9,319 MW**
  - Neighboring countries (Laos, Myanmar): **6,235 MW** (2024)
- **Transmission:** Owns and operates the national high-voltage transmission grid (500 kV to 69 kV)
- **Sales:** Sells electricity to MEA, PEA, direct large customers, and exports
- **Planning:** Leads long-term power development planning (PDP)
- **Grid Modernization:** Leading smart grid and storage initiatives

**Total contractual capacity managed by EGAT reached 55,832 MW by end of 2024.**

Sources: EGAT Annual Report 2024; EGAT Sustainability Report 2024; Thai BMA Prospectus

### 1.3 PEA — Provincial Electricity Authority

**Role:** Electricity distribution in provincial (non-Bangkok) areas

- Purchases bulk power from EGAT
- Distributes to consumers across all provinces outside Bangkok metro
- Covers approximately 75% of Thailand's geographic area
- Manages local supply, metering, billing, and grid maintenance
- 2025 demand growth forecast: **2.76%**
- Key counterparty for Community Solar PPAs (VSPP programs)

Sources: PEA Annual Report 2024

### 1.4 MEA — Metropolitan Electricity Authority

**Role:** Electricity distribution in Bangkok metropolitan area

- Purchases bulk power from EGAT
- Distributes to consumers in Bangkok, Nonthaburi, and Samut Prakan
- Covers approximately 3,192 square kilometers — the densest load center
- Also a counterparty for solar VSPP PPAs in the metro area

### 1.5 Private Sector Participants

| Category | Description | Capacity Range | Examples |
|----------|-------------|----------------|----------|
| IPP (Independent Power Producer) | Large-scale generators selling to EGAT | >90 MW | Gulf, RATCH, B.Grimm, BLCP |
| SPP (Small Power Producer) | Mid-scale generators; firm and non-firm contracts | 10–90 MW | Firm: gas/coal; Non-firm: solar/wind |
| VSPP (Very Small Power Producer) | Small-scale generators | ≤10 MW | Solar farms, biomass, biogas |

SPPs can sell to both EGAT and MEA directly. VSPPs sell to PEA or MEA under feed-in tariff programs.

### 1.6 Energy Regulatory Commission (ERC)

The **ERC** is the independent regulator overseeing:

- Licensing of power generators (energy industry licenses)
- Tariff regulation and approval
- Grid access rules
- Consumer protection
- One-stop service for solar farm permits (factory license, EIA coordination)

### 1.7 Emerging Market Reforms (2025-2026)

Thailand is moving toward partial market liberalization:

- **Utility Green Tariff (UGT1):** Launched January 2025 — 2,000 GWh/year from EGAT hydropower with RECs from all three utilities. Applications closed February 2025; supply starts mid-2025
- **UGT2:** Expected mid-2025 — expands to solar, wind, battery storage with site selection and 10-year contracts
- **Third-Party Access (TPA):** 2,000 MW pilot from 2025, enabling private grid use for direct power purchase. Grid codes for connection, operation, and service are under development
- **Direct PPA Framework:** Under development for data centers and large industrial consumers
- **Future Direction:** Proposals for multiple buyers (EGAT, MEA, PEA selling directly to users)

Sources: Apala Group Analysis 2025; Krungsri Research; Greater Mekong Subregion Updates

## 2. Electricity Generation Mix

### 2.1 Current Generation Mix (2024)

Thailand remains heavily dependent on natural gas, though renewable energy is growing steadily.

| Source | % of Generation (2024) | TWh (approx.) | Notes |
|--------|----------------------|---------------|-------|
| **Natural Gas** | ~58% | 136.4 TWh | Dominant source; up from 129.4 TWh in 2023 |
| **Coal/Lignite** | ~14% | ~33 TWh | Includes domestic lignite (Mae Moh) and imports |
| **Biofuels/Biomass** | ~6% | ~14 TWh | Leading low-carbon source |
| **Solar** | ~3% | ~7 TWh | Rapidly growing; capacity ~5 GW |
| **Hydropower** | ~3% | ~7 TWh | Domestic + imports from Laos |
| **Wind** | ~2% | ~5 TWh | Growing with new onshore projects |
| **Oil/Diesel** | ~2% | ~5 TWh | Peaking and backup only |
| **Other (MSW, geothermal)** | <1% | <2 TWh | Niche contributions |

**Total Electricity Generated (2024):** ~235 TWh
**Total Installed Capacity:** ~55,832 MW (contractual)
**Peak Demand (2024):** 36.8 GW

### 2.2 Generation Mix Trends

- **Natural gas dominance persists** but is declining slowly. Gas accounted for 65% in 2020 vs. 58% in 2024
- **Renewable share (total):** 13-27% depending on definition (narrow vs. broad including biomass and imports)
- **Low-carbon electricity per capita:** 413 kWh (2024)
- **Total electricity per capita:** 3,254 kWh (2024)
- **Coal is stable** at ~14% — the Mae Moh lignite plant is being gradually converted to biomass
- **Solar growth is accelerating** — approximately 3 GW added in 2024 alone

### 2.3 Capacity vs. Generation

Important distinction for solar developers:

| Source | Installed Capacity (MW) | % of Capacity | Capacity Factor |
|--------|------------------------|---------------|-----------------|
| Natural Gas | ~38,000 | ~68% | ~41% |
| Coal | ~5,000 | ~9% | ~75% |
| Solar | ~5,000 | ~9% | ~16-18% |
| Wind | ~1,500 | ~3% | ~25-30% |
| Hydro | ~3,500 | ~6% | ~23% |
| Biomass | ~3,000 | ~5% | ~50-55% |

Solar has a low capacity factor (~16-18%) compared to gas and coal, meaning installed capacity needs to be much higher to deliver equivalent generation.

### 2.4 2025 Outlook

- **Thermal sources (gas + coal):** Expected to supply ~75% of capacity
- **Renewables:** Solar leads new capacity additions
- **Floating PV tenders:** 205 MWp by 2026 under EGAT floating solar program
- **Demand drivers:** EV adoption increasing electricity demand; data center construction boom

## 3. Electricity Tariff Structure

### 3.1 Overview

Thailand's electricity tariffs are regulated by the ERC and consist of:

1. **Base tariff** (fixed by consumer type and voltage level)
2. **Ft (fuel adjustment charge)** (variable, reviewed quarterly)
3. **Service charge** (fixed monthly)
4. **VAT at 7%** (applied on total)

Tariffs vary by consumer type, voltage level, demand size, and time of use.

### 3.2 Residential Tariffs (PEA Schedule, Base Rate)

Progressive block pricing for residential consumers:

| 16–25 kWh | 2.9882 | Low consumption |
| 26–35 kWh | 3.2405 | |

### 3.7 Regional Price Differences

Thailand does **not** have regional wholesale electricity pricing. The tariff structure is set nationally by the ERC. However, practical differences exist:

- **MEA area (Bangkok)** vs **PEA area (provinces):** Base tariff schedules are nearly identical, but service charges differ slightly
- **Industrial zones** (Eastern Seaboard, Rayong, Chonburi) have concentration of high-voltage industrial tariffs
- **No locational marginal pricing (LMP)** — unlike deregulated markets
- **Grid congestion** in certain areas may affect solar farm grid connection feasibility (e.g., southern provinces)
- **Loss factors** differ by region but are not passed through to tariffs

## 4. Peak vs Off-Peak Pricing (TOU)

### 4.1 Time-of-Use (TOU) Structure

TOU tariffs are available for medium and large commercial/industrial consumers. The structure divides each day into:

| Period | Hours | Days | Pricing |
|--------|-------|------|---------|
| **Peak** | 09:00–22:00 | Monday–Friday | Higher energy + demand charges |
| **Off-Peak** | 22:00–09:00 | Monday–Friday | Lower energy, often no demand charge |
| **Holiday** | 00:00–24:00 | Saturday, Sunday, Public Holidays | Same as off-peak |

### 4.2 TOU Rate Examples (Industrial, PEA)

For a large industrial customer at 22-33 kV (Tariff 4.2):

| Component | Peak | Off-Peak/Holiday |
|-----------|------|-----------------|
| Energy Charge | ~5.0-5.5 THB/kWh | ~2.5-3.0 THB/kWh |
| Demand Charge | ~74-225 THB/kW/mo | None or reduced |

**Peak-to-off-peak ratio:** Approximately **1.8-2.0x**

### 4.3 Implications for Solar Developers

- Solar generation aligns **perfectly** with peak hours (9:00-22:00, especially 10:00-16:00)
- This means solar power displaces the **most expensive** electricity
- TOU structure makes solar economically attractive for industrial self-consumption
- For grid-connected solar farms, the FiT rate is fixed regardless of TOU — but the avoided cost calculation for off-takers favors daytime solar

### 4.4 Residential TOU

Residential consumers default to flat progressive rates (block pricing). TOU is optional and rarely chosen by households. The typical residential consumer does not benefit significantly from TOU due to consumption patterns.

## 5. Ft Fuel Adjustment Mechanism

### 5.1 What is Ft?

The **Ft (fuel adjustment charge)** is Thailand's mechanism to pass through volatile fuel costs to electricity consumers. It is added to (or subtracted from) the base tariff.

**Formula:** Total tariff per kWh = Base rate + Ft + VAT (7%)

### 5.2 How Ft Works

1. **Quarterly Review:** ERC reviews and sets the Ft rate every 4 months (Jan, May, Sep)
2. **Cost Components:** Reflects changes in:
   - Natural gas and LNG prices (dominant driver)
   - Coal import prices
   - Exchange rate fluctuations (fuel is USD-denominated)
   - Power purchase costs from IPPs/SPPs
   - EGAT's generation efficiency
3. **Political Sensitivity:** Ft is frequently subject to government intervention/subsidies to keep electricity affordable
4. **Cross-Subsidy:** EGAT has accumulated losses of ~THB 100 billion from absorbing fuel cost increases not fully passed through

### 5.3 Historical Ft Rates

| Period | Ft Rate (THB/kWh) | Context |
|--------|-------------------|---------|
| 2022 Q4 | +0.9343 | Post-Ukraine crisis; LNG spike |
| 2023 Q1 | +0.9345 | Peak fuel costs |
| 2023 Q3 | +0.70 (approx) | Declining LNG |
| 2024 Q1 | +0.39 (approx) | Government subsidy |
| 2024 Q3 | +0.12 (approx) | Further normalization |
| 2025 Q1 | -0.05 to 0.00 | Near-zero or negative |
| 2025 Q3 | -0.10 (approx) | Projected further decrease |

### 5.4 Relevance to Solar

- **Solar reduces Ft exposure:** Self-consumption solar eliminates the Ft component on generated electricity
- **FiT vs Ft:** Solar farm FiT payments (e.g., 2.25 THB/kWh for community solar) are fixed — they do NOT fluctuate with Ft
- **Competitive advantage:** As gas prices rise, solar becomes more competitive. When gas prices fall, the gap narrows but solar's zero marginal fuel cost remains advantageous long-term

Sources: ERC Quarterly Ft Announcements; Globlex Research; Nation Thailand

- Enables **100 MW** (expandable to 200 MW) of Lao renewable hydropower transmission to Singapore

- Parties: EDL (Laos), EGAT (Thailand), TNB (Malaysia)

### 9.6 ASEAN Power Grid (APG)

- Thailand is central to the ASEAN Power Grid initiative
- Interconnections with Laos, Myanmar, Malaysia, and Cambodia
- ADB technical assistance for trade enhancement
- Long-term vision of a fully integrated ASEAN electricity market

### 9.7 Implications for Solar Developers

- **Import competition:** Cheap Laos hydropower (low-cost PPA) competes with domestic solar
- **Grid capacity:** Cross-border transmission lines consume grid capacity that could be used for solar
- **Opportunity:** Solar + storage can complement hydropower (solar peaks in dry season when hydro drops)
- **Policy risk:** Government may prioritize imports over domestic RE procurement

## 10. Key Takeaways for Solar Developers

### 10.1 Market Opportunity

1. **Massive expansion required:** Thailand needs ~34,000 MW of new solar by 2037 — approximately 2,600 MW/year
2. **Policy support is strong:** PDP 2024, AEDP 2024, NDC 3.0 all prioritize solar
3. **Grid parity achieved:** Solar LCOE (~1.5-2.0 THB/kWh) beats gas generation (~3.5-4.0 THB/kWh)
4. **Community Solar:** 1,500 MW at 2.25 THB/kWh FiT — near-term opportunity
5. **Market reforms:** UGT, TPA, and Direct PPA frameworks opening new revenue streams

### 10.2 Key Risks

1. **Regulatory uncertainty:** Rules change frequently; BOI solar generation incentives ended June 2025
2. **Grid constraints:** EGAT transmission bottlenecks in certain regions
3. **Ft mechanism:** Low gas prices compress solar's relative advantage
4. **Import competition:** Laos hydropower is cheap and politically favored
5. **Foreign ownership limits:** 49% cap unless BOI-promoted

### 10.3 Competitive Positioning

| Factor | Assessment |
|--------|------------|
| Solar irradiation | Excellent (1,600-1,900 kWh/m² GHI) |
| Policy framework | Strong and improving |
| Tariff competitiveness | At or below grid parity |
| Market size | Very large (~34 GW needed) |
| Competitive landscape | Moderate — many Thai developers active |
| Foreign investor access | Moderate — requires local structure |
| Grid access | Challenging — limited capacity in some areas |
| Financing availability | Good — Thai banks and DFIs active in RE |

### 10.4 Recommended Focus Areas

1. **Community Solar (1,500 MW)** — Nearest-term opportunity with defined framework
2. **Corporate PPA / UGT** — Growing demand from industrial/commercial consumers
3. **Solar + Storage** — Increasing demand under PDP 2024; addresses intermittency
4. **Floating Solar** — Less land constraint; EGAT reservoir programs
5. **Agrivoltaics** — Addresses land-use concerns on agricultural land
