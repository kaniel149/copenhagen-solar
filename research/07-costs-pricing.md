Thailand benefits from:
- Lower steel fabrication costs (local production)
- Abundant skilled welders and construction labor
- Competitive galvanized steel supply chain from nearby China
- Estimated **20–35% lower** mounting costs compared to US benchmarks

### 11.2 BOI Investment Promotion Benefits

Thailand's Board of Investment (BOI) offers significant incentives for solar projects:

**Tax Benefits (for promoted activities):**
- **Import duty exemption** on machinery and equipment
- **8-year corporate income tax (CIT) holiday** (15–20% standard rate)
- **50% CIT reduction** for additional 5 years (some categories)
- **100% foreign ownership** permitted
- **Land ownership** rights for promoted companies

**Eligible Activities:**
- Manufacturing of solar cells and raw materials
- Electricity generation from solar energy
- Solar PV rooftop installations
- Battery manufacturing and BESS
- Minimum investment: THB 1 million (excluding land and working capital)

### 11.3 VAT Treatment

| Item | VAT Rate | Notes |
|------|----------|-------|
| Standard VAT | 7% | Applied to most goods and services |
| Imported equipment | 7% (on CIF + duty) | Payable on import |
| BOI-promoted imports | 7% (refundable) | Input VAT credit mechanism |
| Electricity sales | 7% | Applied to PPA revenue |
| Land lease | Exempt | No VAT on land transactions |
| Construction services | 7% | Standard rate |

**Key consideration:** BOI-promoted projects can claim input VAT credits on all imported equipment, effectively making VAT neutral for the project.

### 11.4 US Tariff Impact on Thai Solar Market

In April 2025, the US imposed tariffs of up to **375.2%** on Thai-manufactured solar panels. While this impacts Thai solar manufacturers exporting to the US, it does NOT affect:
- Solar panels imported INTO Thailand for domestic projects
- Thai domestic solar farm development costs
- Solar panels manufactured in China/elsewhere imported to Thailand

**Potential benefit:** Oversupply from redirected Thai manufacturing could lower domestic panel prices.

**Source:** BOI Thailand, Lex Nova Partners, PV KnowHow, Watson Farley & Williams

### 12.2 Currency Impact on Project Costs

| Component | Currency Exposure | Hedging Strategy |
|-----------|------------------|-----------------|
| Solar modules | USD/CNY → THB | 70–80% USD-denominated |
| Inverters | USD/EUR/CNY → THB | 60–70% USD-denominated |
| Mounting (local) | THB | Minimal FX risk |
| Labor | THB | No FX risk |
| Land | THB | No FX risk |
| BESS | USD/CNY → THB | 80%+ USD-denominated |
| Revenue (FiT) | THB | THB-denominated |
| **Net exposure** | | **Revenue in THB, 50–60% of costs in USD** |

### 12.3 Hedging Recommendations

- **Natural hedge:** Local procurement where possible (mounting, labor, civil works)
- **Forward contracts:** Lock in USD/THB for major equipment purchases
- **Project timeline:** Align purchase orders with favorable FX windows
- **Revenue match:** THB revenue covers THB costs naturally
- **Risk:** A 10% THB depreciation increases equipment costs by ~5–6% of total project cost

**Note for Copenhagen (DKK/EUR investors):** Additional EUR/DKK → THB conversion risk. Consider EUR → USD → THB path or direct EUR/THB hedging.
