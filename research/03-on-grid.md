## 1. Overview of Thailand's On-Grid Solar Framework

Thailand's on-grid solar sector operates under a regulated single-buyer model managed by three state utilities:

| Entity | Role | Coverage |
|--------|------|----------|
| **EGAT** (Electricity Generating Authority of Thailand) | Transmission & bulk generation | Nationwide transmission grid |
| **PEA** (Provincial Electricity Authority) | Distribution | 73 provinces (rural/provincial areas) |
| **MEA** (Metropolitan Electricity Authority) | Distribution | Bangkok, Nonthaburi, Samut Prakan |

### Producer Categories

| Category | Capacity | Offtaker |
|----------|----------|----------|
| **VSPP** (Very Small Power Producer) | ≤ 10 MW | PEA or MEA |
| **SPP** (Small Power Producer) | > 10 MW to 90 MW | EGAT |
| **IPP** (Independent Power Producer) | > 90 MW | EGAT |

For solar farm development targeting the PEA grid, the primary pathway is the **VSPP program** (up to 10 MW per connection point) or the **SPP program** for larger installations.

### Key Regulatory Bodies

- **Energy Regulatory Commission (ERC):** Issues regulations on electricity procurement, FiT rates, licensing
- **Energy Policy and Planning Office (EPPO):** Strategic planning, PDP formulation
- **National Energy Policy Council (NEPC):** High-level policy approval (chaired by PM)
- **Department of Alternative Energy Development and Efficiency (DEDE):** RE promotion

### Installed Solar Capacity (as of 2024-2025)

- **Total solar PV:** ~5.2 GW (2025)
- **Rooftop solar:** ~3.6 GWac
- **Ground-mounted/utility-scale:** ~1.6 GW
- **Target under PDP:** 8.7 GW solar by 2037; newer PDP2024 targets 20 GW by 2037

**Source:** IEA PVPS Snapshot 2025; Krungsri Research 2025; TransitionZero 2024

## 2. PEA Grid Connection Process

### Step-by-Step Process for VSPP Solar Farm Connection

#### Step 1: Grid Capacity Assessment (Pre-Application)

Before formal application, developers must verify grid capacity at the intended point of interconnection:

- Submit a request to PEA for grid capacity confirmation
- PEA evaluates the distribution network capacity at the proposed connection point
- Assessment covers voltage level, transformer capacity, and existing connections
- **Timeline:** Typically 30-60 days for capacity confirmation
- **Critical:** Without confirmed grid capacity, no PPA application will be accepted

**Under the 2022-2030 FiT scheme:** Applicants had to submit grid capacity requests to the Electricity Authority by October 28, 2022, with results announced by November 11, 2022.

#### Step 2: ERC PPA Application (via RE Proposal System)

- Submit application through ERC's online **RE Proposal system** (www.erc.or.th)
- For the 2022-2030 round, applications opened November 4-25, 2022
- Application must include:
  - Company registration documents (Thai-registered entity)
  - Proof of 51%+ Thai ownership
  - Grid capacity confirmation from PEA
  - Technical readiness documentation (5 areas — see below)
  - Guarantee deposit (THB 1,000/kW of capacity)

#### Step 3: Technical Readiness Assessment

Applications scored across five areas:

**a. Project Location**
- Land ownership or right to use (options acceptable)
- Compliance with building control and town planning rules
- Detailed plot plans if multi-parcel site

**b. Technical Expertise**
- Plant layout showing PV system, control building, pollution treatment
- Single Line Diagram (SLD) certified by licensed engineer
- Process Flow Diagram
- Prior experience in electricity generation (or affiliates with experience)

**c. Energy Potential**
- Solar irradiation data for the project site
- Resource assessment documentation

**d. Financial**
- Project value estimates
- Bank letter of financial support
- Registered capital ≥ THB 2,000/kW

**e. Planning**
- Environmental report preparation timeline
- Stakeholder engagement plan
- License acquisition schedule
- Finance procurement plan
- Equipment procurement plan
- Construction and interconnection timeline

#### Step 4: PPA Execution

- Qualified applicants announced within ~1 month of application close
- Technical readiness results within ~2.5 months
- Successful projects announced by ERC within ~4 months
- **PPA signing deadline:** Within 120-180 days of ERC notice
- PPA term: **25 years** from SCOD

#### Step 5: Construction and Licensing

- Obtain all required permits:
  - Factory Operating License (รง.4) — being streamlined/exempted for solar from 2025
  - Environmental Impact Assessment (if applicable for larger projects)
  - Building permits
  - Electrical safety certification
- Complete construction per approved design
- Install interconnection equipment per PEA Grid Code

#### Step 6: Grid Connection and Metering

- PEA conducts site inspection to verify:
  - Safe installation per TISI 2572 standards
  - Equipment compliance with ERC Annex 5 and Annex 6
  - Inverter specifications match approved design
  - Protection systems properly configured
- **Bi-directional meter installation** by PEA
- Connection to distribution network

#### Step 7: Testing and Commissioning

- Comprehensive grid connection testing
- Power quality verification
- Protection system testing (anti-islanding, frequency/voltage ride-through)
- Synchronization testing
- Operational guidance provided

#### Step 8: Commercial Operation Date (COD)

- Formal declaration of commercial operation
- FiT payments begin from COD
- 25-year PPA clock starts

### Timeline Summary

| Phase | Duration | Notes |
|-------|----------|-------|
| Grid capacity assessment | 1-2 months | Must be done before PPA application |
| PPA application & review | 3-4 months | Through ERC RE Proposal system |
| PPA signing | 4-6 months from announcement | 120-180 day deadline |
| Licensing & permits | 3-6 months | Parallel with construction preparation |
| Construction | 6-12 months | Depending on project size |
| PEA inspection & connection | 1-2 months | After construction completion |
| Testing & commissioning | 2-4 weeks | Final phase before COD |
| **Total estimated timeline** | **18-30 months** | From initial application to COD |

For hybrid RE projects (e.g., island installations), PEA planning through commissioning takes approximately **6-9 months**.

**Sources:** WFW 2022; ERC Regulations B.E. 2565; PEA Grid Connection Guide; SolarShop Hua Hin Guide

## 3. PPA Structures

### 3.1 Non-Firm PPA (Standard for Solar)

The standard PPA structure for most solar VSPP and SPP projects:

- **Payment basis:** Paid for whatever electricity is generated, up to the MW capacity specified in the PPA
- **No penalty** if generation falls below contracted capacity
- **Applicable to:** Ground-mounted solar (without storage), biogas, wind
- **Offtaker obligation:** Purchase all electricity generated up to contracted capacity
- **Risk profile:** Developer bears construction/technology risk; no output guarantee required
- **Most common** for pure solar farms

### 3.2 Partial-Firm PPA (Solar + Battery Storage)

For ground-mounted solar + battery storage projects only:

**Minimum generation requirements:**

| Time Period | Requirement |
|-------------|-------------|
| **9:00 AM - 4:00 PM** | 100% of MW capacity specified in PPA |
| **6:01 PM - 6:00 AM** | 60% of MW capacity for minimum 2 hours (or more as ordered by offtaker) |
| **Other times** | No minimum; offtaker purchases all generated electricity up to 100% capacity |

- **Higher FiT rate** compensates for storage investment (THB 2.8331 vs 2.1679/kWh)
- **Only available to SPP** (>10 MW to 90 MW) — not VSPP
- **Requires BESS integration** to meet evening dispatch obligations

### 3.3 Firm PPA

- Traditional firm PPAs exist for conventional thermal/gas plants
- **Not typically offered for solar** under current FiT schemes
- May become relevant for large solar+storage+gas hybrid projects

### 3.4 Direct PPA (New Framework — 2024)

Approved by NEPC in June 2024 as a pilot program:

- **Direct sale** from RE producer to BOI-promoted data centers
- **Third-party access (TPA)** to national grid infrastructure
- **Capacity:** Up to 2,000 MW
- **Commercial negotiation** between producer and buyer (not ERC-set tariff)
- **No specific contract form** prescribed by ERC
- **Significance:** First departure from Thailand's single-buyer model
- **Status:** Draft regulations issued; full implementation pending

### 3.5 Private PPA (Corporate PPA)

- Behind-the-meter installations where developer owns the system
- Electricity sold directly to host facility
- No grid feed-in (self-consumption model)
- Growing rapidly in industrial estates
- Examples: GPSC Group's 49 MW portfolio across multiple sites (2012-2024)

**Source:** WFW 2022; Hunton Andrews Kurth 2024; US Commercial Service Thailand 2024

## 4. Feed-in Tariff (FiT) Rates History & Current

### Historical FiT/Adder Evolution

Thailand's solar incentive structure has evolved from generous adder tariffs to lower FiT rates as solar costs declined:

#### Early Period (2007-2014): Adder Tariff System

| Period | Mechanism | Rate | Duration |
|--------|-----------|------|----------|
| 2007-2010 | Solar Adder | THB 8.00/kWh (added to wholesale rate) | 10 years |
| 2010-2013 | Solar Adder (revised) | THB 6.50/kWh | 10 years |
| 2013-2014 | Transition to FiT | Various rates during transition | — |

- The **adder** was paid ON TOP of the wholesale electricity price
- This created extremely attractive returns, driving Thailand's first solar boom
- Projects like Korat 3 and Solarta received THB 8/kWh adder

#### FiT Period (2015-2021): Declining Rates

| Year | Rooftop Solar FiT | Ground-Mount FiT | Notes |
|------|-------------------|-------------------|-------|
| 2015 | THB 6.85/kWh (residential) | THB 5.66/kWh | First formal FiT scheme |
| 2016 | THB 6.85/kWh | THB 4.12/kWh | Continuing decline |
| 2017 | Various by size | Various | Size-differentiated rates |
| 2018 | Various | Various | Agro-Solar phases |
| 2019 | THB 1.68/kWh (commercial) | — | Sharp reduction |
| 2020 | THB 1.68/kWh | — | Maintained low rate |
| 2021 | THB 5.42/kWh (residential pilot) | — | New residential buyback program |

#### Current FiT Scheme (2022-2030): ERC Regulations

Announced October 2022 under ERC Regulations B.E. 2565:

| Project Type | FiT Rate (THB/kWh) | FiT Premium (South Border) | PPA Term |

**FiT Premium:** Additional THB 0.50/kWh for projects in southern border provinces (Yala, Pattani, Narathiwat, and parts of Songkhla).

## 5. Net Metering / Net Billing

### Current System: Net Billing (Not Net Metering)

Thailand uses a **net billing** system, NOT true net metering:

| Feature | Thailand (Net Billing) | True Net Metering |
|---------|----------------------|-------------------|
| Export credit | Fixed buyback rate (lower than retail) | 1:1 kWh offset at retail rate |
| Billing method | Imports billed at retail; exports paid separately | Monthly net consumption offset |
| Meter type | Bi-directional (separate import/export channels) | Bi-directional single meter |
| Prosumer benefit | Encourages self-consumption | Higher value for exports |
| Rollover | No rollover of credits | Often allows monthly rollover |

### Historical Buyback Programs

| Program Period | Target | Buyback Rate | Notes |
|----------------|--------|-------------|-------|
| 2019-2020 | Commercial/Industrial | THB 1.68/kWh | Low rate, limited participation |
| 2021-2024 | Residential ≤10 kW | Up to THB 5.42/kWh | Pilot program, limited slots |
| 2024-present | All categories | THB 2.20/kWh | Standardized rate |

### Key Rules (2024-2025)

1. **Self-consumption priority:** Systems designed primarily for self-use
2. **Export limitations:** Grid feed-in capacity may be limited by utility
3. **No rollover:** Excess generation in one billing period does not carry forward
4. **Capacity limits:** Total solar contract capacity per transformer area limited to <15% of transformer size
5. **Metering:** Digital bi-directional meters required for all grid-connected systems

### Regulatory Changes (2025)

- **December 2024:** Rooftop solar no longer classified as controlled activity requiring licenses
- **November 2025:** Systems up to 10 kW (residential/SME) exempt from licenses/permits if NOT grid-connected
- **Late 2025:** Simple online notification replaces permits for grid-connected residential installs
- **Factory license exemption:** Plants >1 MW no longer need Factory License (รง.4) from 2025

### Tax Incentives (2025)

| Category | Incentive | Conditions |
|----------|-----------|------------|
| Residential | Income tax deduction up to THB 200,000 | VAT invoice required, certified equipment |
| Business | 150% tax deduction | New equipment only, 5-star rated |
| BOI-promoted | Various incentives | Cannot combine with residential/business deductions |

**Source:** CASE Thailand 2025; Tilleke & Gibbins 2024; Krungsri Research 2025; PVKnowHow 2025

## 6. Technical Interconnection Standards

### PEA Grid Code

PEA has established a comprehensive Grid Code for distributed generation interconnection, organized into **4 foundational regulations** covering:

1. **Protection requirements** — Anti-islanding, overcurrent, under/over voltage, under/over frequency
2. **Power quality standards** — Harmonics, voltage flicker, power factor
3. **Network connection specifications** — Physical connection requirements, capacity limits
4. **Operational procedures** — Notification requirements, maintenance, emergency procedures

### Key Technical Standards

| Standard/Code | Description | Applicability |
|---|---|---|
| **PEA Grid Connection Code** | Primary interconnection standard for DG | All VSPP/SPP connected to PEA |
| **TISI 2572** | Thai Industrial Standards Institute - Electrical installation safety | All PV installations |
| **ERC Annex 5** | Equipment specifications and compliance requirements | All ERC-regulated installations |
| **ERC Annex 6** | Inverter specifications for interconnection | Inverters connecting to PEA/MEA grid |
| **IEEE 1547** | Standard for Interconnection of DER | Referenced but not explicitly mandated; PEA codes align with principles |
| **IEC 61727** | PV systems - Characteristics of utility interface | Referenced in inverter requirements |
| **IEC 62116** | Utility-interconnected PV inverters - Test procedure for islanding prevention | Anti-islanding testing |

### Required Documentation

1. **Single Line Diagram (SLD)** — Certified by licensed engineer, showing:
   - PV array configuration
   - Inverter(s) model and rating
   - Protection devices (circuit breakers, fuses, surge protection)
   - Metering points
   - Point of Common Coupling (PCC)
   - Disconnect switches (visible, lockable)

2. **PV Panel Layout Plan** — Showing:
   - Physical location of all panels
   - Orientation and tilt angles
   - String configuration

3. **Inverter Specifications** — Must match PEA/MEA interconnection requirements:
   - Anti-islanding capability
   - Frequency and voltage ride-through per Grid Code
   - Power factor control capability
   - Remote disconnect capability (for larger systems)
   - Compliance with ERC Annex 6

### Inverter Requirements (Summary)

| Parameter | Requirement |
|-----------|-------------|
| Anti-islanding | Must disconnect within 2 seconds of island detection |
| Voltage range | Operating range typically 85-110% of nominal |
| Frequency range | 49.5-50.5 Hz normal operation |
| Power factor | 0.85 leading to 0.85 lagging (adjustable) |
| Harmonic distortion | THD < 5% at rated output |
| DC injection | < 0.5% of rated current |
| Reconnection delay | Minimum 5 minutes after grid restoration |

### Key Compliance Notes

- **No unnotified modifications:** Changes to interconnection equipment require prior PEA approval
- **PEA inspection rights:** PEA reserves the right to inspect at any time for system stability verification
- **Licensed engineer certification:** All major technical documents must be certified by a Thai professional engineer (วิศวกร)
- **Annual compliance:** Periodic inspection may be required for larger installations

**Source:** PEA Grid Code (pea.co.th); IEEE PES Thailand 2017 Dinner Talk; ERC Regulations; TISI Standards

## 7. Metering Types

### 7.1 Bi-Directional Meter (Standard for VSPP)

The primary meter type for all grid-connected solar prosumers and VSPP installations:

**Features:**
- Measures energy flow in both directions (import and export)
- Separate registers for consumption (import) and generation (export)
- Digital display showing cumulative kWh in each direction
- Tamper-resistant design
- Communication capability (in newer AMI-compatible versions)

**Installation:**
- PEA installs the meter as part of grid connection approval
- Cost: Some reports indicate ~THB 8,500 additional fee in certain cases
- Replaces existing standard meter
- Location: At the Point of Common Coupling (PCC) between prosumer and grid

### 7.2 Time-of-Use (TOU) Meters

PEA offers TOU tariff metering for commercial and industrial consumers:

**TOU Rate Structure (PEA typical):**

| Period | Hours | Rate Multiple |
|--------|-------|--------------|
| **On-Peak** | 9:00-22:00 (Mon-Fri) | Higher rate |
| **Off-Peak** | 22:00-9:00 (Mon-Fri), weekends, holidays | Lower rate |

**Relevance to Solar:**
- TOU meters can be combined with bi-directional metering
- Solar generation during on-peak hours offsets higher-rate consumption
- Particularly beneficial for commercial/industrial prosumers
- Smart meters under AMI rollout support TOU natively

### 7.3 Advanced Metering Infrastructure (AMI) / Smart Meters

PEA's smart grid rollout includes next-generation meters:

**Pattaya City Smart Grid Project (Flagship):**
- 13,844 three-phase smart meters
- 102,464 one-phase smart meters
- Total: 116,308 meters covering 120,000 households
- Budget: ~THB 1,069 million (~$27.86 million)

**Smart Meter Features:**
- Two-way communication (PEA can read remotely)
- Real-time consumption monitoring via mobile app and web portal
- Anti-tampering detection
- Load control capability
- Detailed demand data (15-minute intervals)
- Support for TOU and dynamic pricing
- Integration with demand response programs

### 7.4 VSPP Metering Requirements

For VSPP solar farms connected to PEA:

| Metering Point | Meter Type | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| **Point of Common Coupling (PCC)** | Bi-directional | Measure net energy exchange with grid |
| **Generator output** | Production meter | Track total generation (for FiT calculation) |
| **Auxiliary consumption** | Standard meter | Measure plant self-consumption |

**Source:** PEA Smart Grid documentation; SolarShop Hua Hin; CASE Thailand 2025; PEA Annual Report 2022

## 8. Grid Capacity Constraints by Region

### Overview

Thailand's grid, managed by PEA in provincial areas, faces varying capacity constraints that affect solar farm connections. Grid capacity is the single most critical bottleneck for new solar development.

### Regional Capacity Assessment

| Region | Key Characteristics | Solar Potential | Grid Constraints |
|--------|-------------------|-----------------|------------------|
| **Central** (incl. EEC) | 34.62% of installed solar; industrial load base; highest grid density | High irradiation; strong demand match | **Moderate** — strong backbone grid but saturated in industrial zones |
| **Northeast** (Isan) | Agricultural area; growing solar adoption; lower demand density | **Highest irradiation** in Thailand (~5.0-5.5 kWh/m²/day); vast land availability | **High** — weaker distribution grid; long transmission distances |
| **North** | Mountain terrain; EGAT floating solar at dams; agricultural | Good irradiation; limited flat land | **High** — remote areas; weak distribution; flooding concerns |
| **East** (EEC) | 27.85% of installed solar; export-oriented factories | High demand; co-located generation | **Moderate** — new investment in grid infrastructure |
| **South** | Tourism-driven demand; island infrastructure challenges | Lower irradiation than NE; strong coastal winds | **Variable** — mainland moderate; islands severe |

### Specific Bottlenecks

**Northeast Thailand (Isan):**
- Highest solar irradiation but weakest grid infrastructure
- Long-distance 115 kV transmission lines from Central region
- Many substations at or near capacity
- Agro-photovoltaic projects (up to 800 MW by 2028) will stress grid further
- Distribution centers newly opened in Ubon Ratchathani and Khon Kaen signal anticipated growth

**Southern Thailand:**
- Island infrastructure fundamentally constrained (submarine cables, diesel generation)
- Mainland grid adequate for moderate solar development
- FiT premium (+THB 0.50/kWh) for southern border provinces incentivizes development in areas with weaker grid

**Central Thailand:**
- Best-developed grid infrastructure
- Competition for connection points due to industrial density
- Floating solar development at reservoirs (Srinakarin Dam: 158 MW) adds capacity without distribution stress

### Grid Connection Priority (2022-2030 Scheme)

When multiple applications target the same connection point with capacity limitations, technologies are prioritized:

1. **Biogas (wastewater/waste)** — Highest priority
2. **Wind** — Second priority
3. **Ground-mounted solar + battery storage** — Third priority
4. **Ground-mounted solar** — Lowest priority

This means pure solar projects are most at risk of being displaced at congested connection points.

### Grid Development Plans

EGAT's grid expansion includes:
- **200.52 km of new 115 kV lines** added in 2025
- **230 kV submarine cable to Koh Samui** (11.23 billion baht, operational 2028-2029)
- Smart grid investments across PEA distribution network
- AMI rollout enabling better demand management

**Source:** Mordor Intelligence 2025; TransitionZero 2024; PVKnowHow 2024; Krungsri Research 2025

## 9. Curtailment Rules

### Current Framework

Thailand does **not have explicit, published curtailment rules** for solar generation in the way that markets like Germany, China, or California do. Key points:

**What exists:**
- PEA may impose **local limits on net metering or feed-in** to avoid congestion at specific transformers or substations
- Total solar contract capacity per transformer area limited to **<15% of transformer nameplate capacity**
- VSPP connection agreements include provisions for PEA to disconnect generation during grid emergencies
- Partial-firm PPA projects (solar+BESS) may be curtailed if they cannot meet dispatch obligations

**What doesn't exist (yet):**
- No published province-level solar curtailment quotas
- No dynamic curtailment thresholds or real-time curtailment signals
- No mandatory curtailment compensation mechanism
- No centralized renewable energy curtailment monitoring

### Practical Curtailment Scenarios

| Scenario | Likely Response | Impact on Developer |
|----------|----------------|-------------------|
| Local transformer overload | PEA refuses new connections; may disconnect existing | Revenue loss; potential PPA breach |
| Transmission congestion | EGAT dispatches down; affects SPP more than VSPP | Reduced generation payment |
| Grid frequency deviation | Inverter auto-disconnect per Grid Code | Brief interruption; no penalty |
| Grid emergency | PEA manual disconnection | No penalty; force majeure |
| Excess renewable penetration | Policy discussion ongoing; no formal mechanism | Future risk |

### Future Direction

- **PDP2024** acknowledges need for curtailment management as renewables scale to 33% by 2030
- Smart grid investments (AMI, SCADA) will enable better real-time management
- BESS deployment (10 GW target by 2037) intended to absorb excess solar
- Direct PPA framework may create alternative offtake reducing curtailment risk

**Source:** PEA documentation; CASE Thailand 2025; UNDP Thailand Report 2025; Ember Energy 2025

## 10. SCOD Requirements and Penalties

### Scheduled Commercial Operation Date (SCOD)

The SCOD is specified in the PPA and represents the date by which the project must begin commercial electricity sales.

**Under the 2022-2030 FiT Scheme:**

| Year | Solar+BESS SCOD | Wind SCOD | Solar Only SCOD | Biogas SCOD |
|------|----------------|-----------|-----------------|-------------|
| 2024 | ✓ | — | ✓ | ✓ |
| 2025 | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ |
| 2026 | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ |
| 2027 | ✓ | ✓ | — | ✓ |
| 2028 | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ |
| 2029 | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | — |
| 2030 | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | — |

### Pre-COD Requirements

Before achieving COD, VSPP developers must:

1. **Obtain all required licenses** (factory license, electrical license, etc.)
2. **Install compliant equipment** per ERC Annex specifications
3. **Connect to grid** following PEA Power Network Regulations
4. **Complete all testing** and receive PEA sign-off
5. **Notify distributor** of readiness for commercial operation

### Extension Provisions

- Developers must notify PEA/MEA **in advance** if unable to meet SCOD
- Must provide details on:
  - Construction status
  - Funding status
  - Technology readiness
  - License status
- **One extension possible:** Up to 1 month, at distributor's discretion
- Extension does not affect PPA termination date

### Penalty Structure

| Milestone | Penalty |
|-----------|---------|
| **SCOD + 60 days:** Unable to supply electricity | Daily fine of **0.33% of guarantee payment** begins |
| **SCOD + 360 days:** Still unable to supply | **PPA terminated**; full performance guarantee forfeited |
| **Failure to sign PPA** within deadline | PPA voided; guarantee forfeited |

### Guarantee Requirements

| Item | Amount | Form |
|------|--------|------|
| Application guarantee | THB 1,000/kW | Bank guarantee or cash |
| Performance guarantee | THB 1,000/kW (minimum) | Bank guarantee |
| **Example:** 10 MW VSPP | THB 10 million (~$270,000) | Must be liquid/immediately available |

### Force Majeure

- Force majeure or government fault may excuse SCOD delays
- Definition typically includes: natural disasters, war, government actions, epidemics
- Does NOT include: financing difficulties, equipment delays, labor shortages
- Must be documented and approved by PEA/ERC

**Source:** ERC Regulations B.E. 2565; Tilleke & Gibbins 2025; WFW 2022; Asia Pacific Energy Policy

## 12. Differences Between PEA Regions

### PEA Regional Structure

PEA is organized into **12 Regional Offices** covering 73 provinces, grouped into 4 macro-regions:

### Regional Comparison

| Factor | Central | Northeast (Isan) | North | South |
|--------|---------|------------------|-------|-------|
| **Solar irradiation** | 4.5-5.0 kWh/m²/day | 5.0-5.5 kWh/m²/day (highest) | 4.5-5.0 kWh/m²/day | 4.0-4.8 kWh/m²/day |
| **% of installed solar** | ~35% | Growing (SiS new centers) | ~10-15% (floating solar) | Minimal |
| **Grid strength** | Strongest | Weakest distribution | Moderate; remote areas weak | Variable; islands severe |
| **Land availability** | Limited (urbanized) | Abundant (agricultural) | Limited (mountainous) | Limited (tourism/coastal) |
| **Demand profile** | Industrial + commercial | Agricultural; low evening load | Mixed; tourism in north | Tourism-driven; seasonal |
| **Key projects** | Solarta (34 MW), Lopburi (73 MW) | Korat 3 (6 MW), agro-PV pilots | EGAT floating solar (205 MW) | Island microgrids |
| **Connection ease** | Moderate (competition) | Difficult (weak grid) | Difficult (terrain) | Difficult (infrastructure) |
| **FiT premium** | No | No | No | Yes (+0.50/kWh in border provinces) |
| **Growth trajectory** | Steady | Fastest growth potential | Dam-based floating solar | Island/microgrid focused |

### Specific Regional Considerations

**Central Region (PEA Regions 1-3):**
- Best grid infrastructure; most connection points available
- High competition for grid connection — oversaturated in some industrial zones
- Floating solar at Srinakarin Dam (158 MW) — significant pipeline
- 91 MW acquisition in Suphanburi/Kanchanaburi (2025)
- Closest to load centers; least transmission loss

**Northeast Region (PEA Regions 4-5):**
- **Best solar resource** in the country
- Vast available agricultural land at low cost
- Grid infrastructure is the main constraint
- New PEA service centers opening (Ubon Ratchathani, Khon Kaen)
- Agro-photovoltaic projects up to 800 MW planned by 2028
- **Opportunity:** First-mover advantage in areas with available grid capacity

**Northern Region (PEA Regions 6-7):**
- Mae Hong Son Smart Grid Pilot (EGAT) — 3 MW solar + 4 MW BESS (COD May 2023)
- Floating solar at Bhumibol Dam (205 MW) and Sirikit Dam
- Mountainous terrain limits ground-mount options
- Remote communities benefit from microgrid solutions
- Mae Sariang microgrid (PEA) — THB 265 million budget

**Southern Region (PEA Regions 8-9):**
- FiT premium incentivizes development in border provinces
- Island electricity challenges drive microgrid/hybrid innovation
- Tourism load creates evening peak demand (opposite solar generation peak)
- Submarine cable projects (Koh Samui, Koh Phangan, Koh Tao) expand grid reach
- Koh Lipe: 3.5 MW solar + 7.3 MWh BESS (flagship island project, 2025)

### PEA Processing Times (Anecdotal)

Processing times and administrative efficiency vary by regional office. Developer reports suggest:

- **Central PEA offices:** Most experienced with solar applications; relatively faster processing
- **Northeast offices:** Growing experience; may require more follow-up
- **Northern offices:** Smaller teams; personalized but slower
- **Southern offices:** Variable; island-related applications require additional coordination

## 13. Community Solar Program (2025-2026)

### Overview

Approved October 27, 2025, by the ERC as part of the Ministry of Energy's **"Quick Big Win"** policy:

**Key Parameters:**
- **Total national capacity:** 1,500 MW
- **Maximum per site:** 10 MW (VSPP)
- **Installation type:** Ground-mounted only (not rooftop)
- **Structure:** One project per local community; one VSPP per grid connection point
- **Operator:** VSPP entity (community-linked)
- **PPA type:** ฿2.25/kWh for 25-year term (ERC regulations announced late 2025)

**Objectives:**
1. Boost local energy security
2. Reduce community electricity costs
3. Create local employment and investment
4. Support Thailand's renewable energy targets

**Timeline:**
- October 2025: Framework approved
- November 2025: Draft ERC procurement regulations
- December 2025: Applications open
- January 2026: Target full rollout (within 4 months)

**Licensing Relief:**
- Exemption from factory operation licenses (รง.4) under consideration
- Simplified permitting pathway for community projects

**Significance for Developers:**
- 1,500 MW is a massive quota — larger than annual allocations under the FiT scheme
- Community partnership requirement creates a new stakeholder dynamic
- Ground-mounted focus means land acquisition/leasing is key
- One project per community limits geographic clustering

**Source:** Hunton Andrews Kurth 2025; ERC announcements; Krungsri Research 2025

## 14. Direct PPA Framework (2024-2025)

### Background

Approved by the National Energy Policy Council (NEPC) in June 2024:

**Key Features:**
- First departure from Thailand's enhanced single-buyer model
- Allows RE producers to sell **directly** to qualifying consumers
- Uses **Third-Party Access (TPA)** to national grid
- Initial target: **BOI-promoted data centers only**
- Capacity: Up to **2,000 MW**

**Structure:**
- Commercial negotiations between producer and buyer
- No specific contract form prescribed by ERC
- Grid usage fees (wheeling charges) apply
- Producer must be licensed by ERC
- Buyer must hold BOI promotion certificate

**Draft Regulations:**
- ERC issued draft regulations for public comment
- Key issues: wheeling charges, grid access rules, metering, settlement
- Full implementation timeline: Pending as of early 2026

**Implications:**
- Opens corporate PPA market for renewable energy
- Data centers driving initial demand (energy-intensive, 24/7 load)
- Could expand beyond data centers if pilot succeeds
- Creates price discovery mechanism outside FiT
- Potential for higher returns than FiT for well-located projects

**Source:** Hunton Andrews Kurth 2024; US Commercial Service Thailand 2024; W.Media 2024; Krungsri Research 2025

## 15. Key Takeaways for Developers

### Opportunities

1. **Massive quota availability:** 5,203 MW under FiT (2022-2030) + 1,500 MW community solar + 2,000 MW Direct PPA
2. **Regulatory simplification:** 2025 changes ease permitting significantly
3. **Solar+BESS premium:** 30% higher FiT rate (2.8331 vs 2.1679 THB/kWh) for storage-equipped projects
4. **Southern border premium:** Additional THB 0.50/kWh incentive
5. **Community solar:** New 1,500 MW program creating fresh opportunities
6. **Direct PPA:** Corporate offtake at potentially higher rates than FiT

### Challenges

1. **Grid capacity:** Single biggest bottleneck, especially in Northeast and South
2. **51% Thai ownership:** Limits foreign investment structures
3. **Guarantee requirements:** THB 1,000-2,000/kW ties up significant capital
4. **Net billing (not metering):** Low buyback rates discourage pure export models
5. **SCOD penalties:** 0.33%/day fines and PPA termination risk
6. **No clear curtailment framework:** Uncertainty for high-penetration scenarios
7. **PPA queue:** Oversubscription likely for solar at popular connection points

### Strategic Recommendations

1. **Secure grid capacity early** — This is the most critical bottleneck
2. **Consider solar+BESS** for higher FiT and grid priority
3. **Explore community solar** — Fresh 1,500 MW quota with potentially faster timeline
4. **Northeast offers best irradiation** — But verify grid capacity at specific sites
5. **Partner with Thai entity** for 51%+ ownership requirement
6. **Build relationships with local PEA offices** — Processing varies by region
7. **Budget for guarantees** — THB 10-20M+ for 10 MW project
8. **Monitor Direct PPA expansion** — May open better returns for large projects
